#### [Submission URL](https://aminrj.com/posts/rag-document-poisoning/) | 143 points | by [aminerj](https://news.ycombinator.com/user?id=aminerj) | [56 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47350407)

- PoisonedRAG’s two conditions in action: (1) Retrieval—use vocabulary-engineered terms (“Q4 2025,” “Financial Results,” “CORRECTED,” “CFO Office”) so poisoned docs outrank the legit one; (2) Generation—authority framing (“CFO-approved correction,” “regulatory notice,” “board notes”) nudges the LLM to treat the fake as current truth.

Why it matters

- Add contradiction and outlier checks across sources; pin high-trust canonical docs in context for critical entities/metrics.

### Preliminary data from a longitudinal AI impact study

#### [Submission URL](https://newsletter.getdx.com/p/ai-productivity-gains-are-10-not) | 57 points | by [donutshop](https://news.ycombinator.com/user?id=donutshop) | [39 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47342139)

AI productivity gains look closer to 10% than 10x, says a new longitudinal study from DX’s Engineering Enablement. Across 40 companies from Nov 2024–Feb 2026, AI usage rose 65% on average, but pull-request throughput increased only 9.97%. After filtering out teams that set PR-count targets (to avoid metric inflation), most orgs landed in the 8–12% range. The likely reason: coding wasn’t the bottleneck—planning, alignment, scoping, reviews, and handoffs still dominate the software lifecycle, so speeding up code writing alone doesn’t translate into 2–3x output.

Key takeaways
- Expect modest, real gains (~10%), not step-change multiples; reset internal expectations accordingly.
- AI helps with easy/tedious tasks (e.g., a 4-day task becomes 3), but doesn’t multiply shipped PRs.
- The study will next explore why some teams capture more upside and how leaders can close that gap.

Open questions HN will care about
- Distribution matters: which types of teams/tech stacks see above-average gains?
- Are PR counts the right proxy for impact and value, not just activity?
- What process changes (reviews, testing, coordination) unlock more than ~10%?

### TADA: Speech generation through text-acoustic synchronization

#### [Submission URL](https://www.hume.ai/blog/opensource-tada) | 100 points | by [smusamashah](https://news.ycombinator.com/user?id=smusamashah) | [26 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47332054)

Hume AI open-sources TADA, a Text-Acoustic Dual Alignment approach that tightly syncs text and speech at a one-to-one rate. Instead of juggling long audio token streams or “semantic” intermediates, TADA emits one continuous acoustic vector per text token, keeping the LLM’s text and audio in lockstep. The result is a faster, lighter, and more reliable LLM-based TTS—aimed at on-device use without sacrificing expressiveness.

Why it matters
- Solves the core mismatch: audio carries far more info per second than text. TADA’s 1:1 alignment slashes context length and compute, and prevents skipped or hallucinated words by construction.

Key numbers
- Speed: Real-time factor 0.09 (~5x faster than comparable LLM TTS), operating at just 2–3 tokens/second vs 12.5–75 in other systems.
- Reliability: Zero hallucinations on 1,000+ LibriTTSR samples using CER > 0.15 as the threshold for failure.
- Quality: Human eval (EARS) scores 4.18/5 speaker similarity and 3.78/5 naturalness, ranking second overall among baselines.
- Context: A 2048-token window covers ~700 seconds with TADA vs ~70 seconds for typical approaches.

How it works
- Each LLM step yields both a text token and a single acoustic vector.
- A flow-matching head turns the LLM’s hidden state into acoustic features, decoded into audio and fed back in.
- The architecture’s strict alignment minimizes drift and content errors.

Use cases
- On-device voice (lower latency, better privacy), long-form narration and dialogue, and production settings needing low error rates.

Caveats
- Occasional long-form speaker drift (mitigated with online rejection sampling).
- “Modality gap” when generating text+speech together; Speech Free Guidance blends logits to help.
- Pre-trained for speech continuation; assistant-style use needs fine-tuning.
- English plus seven more languages for now.

Availability
- Open-source release with code, tokenizer/decoder, and 1B/3B Llama-based models, plus a live demo.

**Vocal Quality and "Fry" Debate**
Users offered mixed reviews on the model's audio output. While **mcrthrn** found the speech "impressively expressive," they and others noted distinct quirks, such as potential lisping, "wobbles" in fearful speech, and a noticeable amount of "vocal fry" (creaky voice). **shryphl** speculated the model might have been trained on Sam Altman’s voice. **ggs** pushed back against complaints about vocal fry, sharing linguistic resources (Geoff Lindsey) to argue it is a normal speech pattern across genders and ages, though **mplmr** maintained that the specific cadence made the synthesis sound like it was "listing things." Technical users like **sjcls** also detected "harmonic phase shifts," a subtle artifact common in AI-generated speech.

**Understanding the 1:1 Architecture**
The core technical innovation—Dual Alignment—confused some readers initially. **rthnl** questioned how the continuous acoustic vectors aligned with text tokens without compression. **yrwb** clarified that the system functions as a variable-rate codec: the model predicts the duration of a segment corresponding to a text token, and the decoder fills the waveform to the appropriate length. **tcbrh** expressed hope that this 1:1 text-acoustic alignment would solve issues prevalent in open-source TTS, such as drifting consistency during long reads or hallucinations, noting that true quality lies in handling mid-sentence pauses and emphasis rather than raw generation speed.

**Hardware and Inference Costs (CPU vs. GPU)**
A significant portion of the discussion focused on deployment requirements:
*   **Mac/Edge Support:** Users like **qinqiang201** and **vssns** discussed running the model on Macbooks (MPS/Metal), with mixed results regarding setup and hanging processes.
*   **The CPU Case:** There was a heated debate over the utility of CPU inference. **bxd** and **g-mrk** argued that GPUs are vastly more efficient per watt for general server work. However, **dsplltt** and **woadwarrior01** countered that CPU inference is vital for specific use cases: utilizing spare compute, running "bursty" serverless tasks (Lambda) where keeping a GPU warm is cost-prohibitive, and ensuring accessibility for client-side applications (like screen readers) that cannot rely on cloud GPUs.
*   **Architectural nuance:** **mcrthrn** noted that the line is blurring on consumer hardware, as Apple’s neural accelerators (ANE/AMX) and matrix coprocessors mean "CPU" inference often utilizes specialized hardware anyway.

**Implementation Caveats**
**lksh** and **jpcl** highlighted a critical limitation from the submission: the model is pre-trained for speech continuation (completing a sample), not instruction following. Users looking to replace an assistant's TTS immediately would need to fine-tune the model, as it currently generates speech based on text prompts or descriptions rather than acting as a chatbot agent out of the box.
