**Marketing Claims vs. Real-World Brittleness**
Users expressed concern over the gap between enterprise marketing and the paper's findings. With tech giants pitching LLMs as capable of handling accounting, crunching numbers, and closing deals, commenters warned that "machine confabulation" is a major risk. Because LLMs make subtle, confident errors that humans struggle to notice, deploying them in complex, multi-step environments degrades their reliability exponentially as context length grows.

**Are We Testing LLMs Fairly?**
Some commenters pushed back against the paper's "zero-error" premise. **The Takeaway:** 
The Hacker News community largely agrees with the paper's underlying premise—models have brittle edges—but argues the solution isn't to force LLMs to do math natively. CVE-2026-4747: Remote kernel RCE in FreeBSD’s NFS RPCSEC_GSS (kgssapi.ko)

What happened
- A stack buffer overflow in FreeBSD’s RPCSEC_GSS server-side validation lets an attacker achieve remote kernel code execution via NFS, yielding uid 0.
- Affected: FreeBSD 13.5 (<p11), 14.3 (<p10), 14.4 (<p1), 15.0 (<p5). Tested on 14.4-RELEASE amd64 (GENERIC, no KASLR).
- Advisory: FreeBSD-SA-26:08.rpcsec_gss.

Root cause
- In svc_rpc_gss_validate(), the code reconstructs an RPC header into a 128-byte on-stack buffer, then memcpy’s the RPCSEC_GSS credential body without checking its length.
- Only 96 bytes are actually available after fixed header fields; larger credentials overflow into saved registers and the return address.
- The fix adds a bounds check that rejects credentials exceeding the remaining buffer space.

Reachability and constraints
- Trigger path: RPCSEC_GSS auth (flavor 6) with GSS DATA on NFS (port 2049/TCP), after a valid context lookup and replay check.
- Crucially, the attacker must possess a valid Kerberos ticket for the NFS service principal; without it the code bails out earlier and the memcpy isn’t hit.
- XDR caps credential size at 400 bytes, leaving enough headroom for a reliable overflow.
- The write-up demonstrates a working remote exploit that pivots to a root shell.

Why it matters
- This is a remotely reachable kernel-level RCE in a widely deployed network service, especially in enterprise environments using NFS with Kerberos.
- Even low-privilege users with valid Kerberos tickets can compromise the server.

What to do
- Patch immediately: 13.5-p11, 14.3-p10, 14.4-p1, 15.0-p5 (or newer).
- Interim mitigations if you can’t patch fast:
  - Disable NFS Kerberos (RPCSEC_GSS) or unload kgssapi.ko where possible.

### Do LLMs Break the Sapir-Whorf Hypothesis?

#### [Submission URL](https://dnhkng.github.io/posts/sapir-whorf/) | 12 points | by [dot_treo](https://news.ycombinator.com/user?id=dot_treo) | [6 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47591834)

- The claim: In transformer LLMs, “thought” and “language” are functionally separate. Language acts as input/output; reasoning happens in a language-agnostic middle space. If true, it undercuts both strong and weak Sapir–Whorf (at least for models).

- How they got there:
  - Prior work (LLM Neuroanatomy Part I): Simply duplicating a block of middle layers in Qwen2-72B (no retraining) produced a big jump on the HuggingFace Open LLM Leaderboard. Duplicating early or late layers broke the model. This led to a three-phase anatomy hypothesis:
    1) Encoding (early): tokens → internal representation
    2) Reasoning (middle): format-agnostic “meaning space”
    3) Decoding (late): internal representation → tokens
  - Part II: RYS (Repeat Your Self) generalizes across models/sizes; evidence, building on Evan Maunder’s work, that middle layers operate in a language-independent space.
  - New experiment: Feed semantically matched sentences across eight languages (EN, FR, ZH, JA, KO, AR, RU, HI) and eight topics (science, poetry, history, cooking, law, medicine, sports, economics). Probe internal activations to test whether mid-layer representations align by meaning rather than by surface language.

- Key findings:
  - The reasoning layers cluster by topic/meaning across languages, not by script or family.
  - Early layers reflect surface form; late layers reflect generation dynamics. Only the middle supports RYS gains without breaking behavior.
  - The pattern replicates across four architecturally different models.

- Why it matters:
  - Suggests LLM “cognition” can be language-agnostic, with language as an interface rather than the substrate of thought.
  - Offers a mechanistic counterexample to Sapir–Whorf within artificial systems.
  - Practical upshot: scaling or selectively amplifying middle layers can boost reasoning without touching vocabulary or decoding—useful for efficiency and targeted upgrades.
  - Interpretability: strengthens a clean separation of concerns for probing and steering models.

- Caveats and open questions:
  - This is about transformers, not humans; it doesn’t settle Sapir–Whorf in cognitive science.
  - “Reasoning” here means internal representation that’s less tied to surface form; whether it maps to humanlike thought is debatable.
  - More rigorous causal tests and tasks beyond short declaratives would help validate the language-agnostic claim.
  - How this interacts with multilingual pretraining balance, tokenization quirks, and non-Latin scripts at scale remains to be fully mapped.

Bottom line: Across multiple LLMs, the middle of the stack appears to represent meaning in a language-independent space, with language relegated to I/O—an architectural result that, for models at least, breaks the assumed entanglement behind Sapir–Whorf.
