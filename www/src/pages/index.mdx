#### [Submission URL](https://novaberg.de/papers/clipboard-pattern.html) | 21 points | by [ClausVomBerg](https://news.ycombinator.com/user?id=ClausVomBerg) | [5 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48099171)

TL;DR
Claus Schlehhuber argues that the default “agents talk to each other in prose” pattern is an architectural anti‑pattern. Every hop re-encodes meaning, burns tokens, and defeats unit testing. His fix: a single, shared, typed state object — a “clipboard” — that flows through a LangGraph. Each specialist node reads the fields it needs, writes structured results back, and hands the clipboard on. Transport data completely; let the consumer format.

What’s new
- Names and sharpens a practice many teams have converged on: replace natural‑language message passing with a strongly typed, shared state (TypedDict schema) inside a graph.
- Introduces a clean, scalable taxonomy — Roles, Departments, Graphs — for composing many specialists without devolving into prompt spaghetti.
- Open implementation notes in LangGraph; Apache 2.0.

Why it matters
- Semantic drift: Each agent rewording another agent’s text accumulates error; intent turns into rumor after a few hops.
- Token cost: You repeatedly pay to read and rewrite the same facts in paragraphs instead of a few schema fields.
- Testability: Strings-in/strings-out agents resist unit tests. A typed clipboard enables fixtures and precise assertions on fields and provenance.

How it works
- One shared “case file” (clipboard) moves from node to node. Nodes:
  - Read only the typed fields they care about.
  - Write results to specific fields (with types, structure, and provenance).
  - Never summarize each other’s outputs in prose.
- At the end, you format from the clipboard once for the user or downstream system.

Context and critique
- Calls out CrewAI, AutoGen, and common LangChain “agent-supervisor” templates for defaulting to NL message passing; says the fix isn’t better prompts but a different architecture.
- This is essentially a modernized blackboard architecture: not novel, but well articulated and named for today’s LLM tooling.

Trade-offs and gotchas
- Upfront schema design, versioning, and migrations add discipline (and friction).
- Concurrency/merging become real design questions if multiple branches update the clipboard.
- You still need robust parsing/validation to coerce model output into the schema.
- Best fit for pipeline/graph workflows; free-form “chatty” collaboration is intentionally de-emphasized.

Sound bite
“Multi-agent systems shouldn’t gossip. They should share a case file.”

For practitioners
- If your agents are summarizing each other’s summaries, expect drift, higher spend, and opaque failures.
- Move to a typed shared state, add validators, track provenance per field, and make agents unit-testable with fixtures.
- Keep natural language at the edges (I/O); keep structure inside the system.

AI chatbots are the new homepage carousel. In a wry field note from client work, the author traces a decade of me-too web trends—carousels, cookie banners, Tag Manager—to today’s default: a blinking chatbot in the corner. Clients rarely use chatbots themselves and often find them annoying or wrong, yet still want one because it signals modernity. “The chatbot has become a social signal, not a tool.”

Attempts to sell a fast, minimal “smolweb” alternative land well—until “simple” is read as “not impressive.” The hard, invisible work of clarity and speed doesn’t look expensive; a chatbot does. The piece argues the pressure comes less from clients than from a web culture that’s normalized bloat and feature theater. Any shift may come from users who notice the calm site just works. Until then, the chatbot stays—blinking, unhelpful, but present, because everyone else has one.

**Hacker News Daily Digest: The Chatbot Illusion & The Modern Web's Feature Theater**

**The Premise:** 
A featured field note sparked a massive discussion today by declaring that AI chatbots are the new "homepage carousel"—a bloated, often useless feature driven entirely by web culture’s obsession with signaling modernity. Clients reject fast, minimalist "smolweb" designs because simplicity doesn't look expensive, whereas a blinking corner chatbot feels like a tangible return on investment, even if nobody uses it. 

**The Hacker News Discussion:**
The HN community strongly resonated with the author's frustration, expanding the conversation into the psychology of clients, the demise of the usable web, and a rising paranoia about AI-generated content. 

Here are the top takeaways from the discussion:

*   **FOMO and "Monkey-See, Monkey-Do" Web Design:**
    The consensus is that chatbots are driven entirely by executive FOMO (Fear Of Missing Out). Commenters likened the modern chatbot to the waving "Under Construction" GIFs of the 90s. Marketing and AI companies are playing heavily on this fear—pushing an "adopt or be left behind" narrative. As one user noted, if you ask a client what a chatbot will actually do that a good FAQ page can't, the honest answer is usually just: "It looks modern."
*   **The $2,000 Bot with Zero Users:**
    Several developers shared anecdotes validating the original article. One user highlighted a nonprofit that paid consultants to build a bot, only to be surprised by an ongoing $2,000/month API bill. When they finally checked the analytics, there were exactly *zero* legitimate user conversations happening. Some joked that developers should just build mock chatbots with pre-generated wrong answers, as no one would notice the difference. 
*   **Simplicity is Hard; Plugins are Easy:**
    The thread highlighted a painful irony for developers: building a genuinely fast, restrained, and highly functional website is incredibly difficult, but it goes unappreciated. Bolting on a WordPress plugin for a chatbot is easy and makes a site "look complete" to stakeholders. Many commenters pointed to **McMaster-Carr** as the holy grail of utilitarian, efficient web design that clients should aspire to, rather than chasing flashy popups.
*   **User Hostility and Bot Jailbreaking:**
    Users are exhausted by the modern web's dark patterns. Commenters vented about chatbots that block page layouts on mobile, fake 12x12 pixel close buttons, and endless layered pop-ups. In response, "banner blindness" is evolving into "bot blindness." A few users even admitted that their new micro-hobby is acting hostile toward forced chatbots or trying to jailbreak customer support AI.
*   **The Meta-Debate: AI Witch Hunts:**
    In a fascinatingly ironic twist, a large portion of the thread devolved into a debate over whether the submitted article—and several comments within the thread—were actually written by LLMs. Users pointed out bland, "human-washed" LLM syntax, leading to accusations and counter-examples where users actively prompted LLMs to recreate the blog post to prove it could be faked. It highlighted the web's current "low-trust environment."

**The Ultimate Takeaway:** 
While a few users noted that well-integrated bots trained on complex corporate data (like Salesforce setups) actually *do* provide value, the vast majority view front-page chatbots as feature bloat. The general prediction for the future? As automated noise reaches a fever pitch, the ultimate "premium" innovation in three years will just be a phone number staffed by a real human being.

### People Hate AI Art

#### [Submission URL](https://mccue.dev/pages/5-8-26-ai-art) | 148 points | by [3dedb728-3f77](https://news.ycombinator.com/user?id=3dedb728-3f77) | [170 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48070548)

Don’t use AI art if you care how people perceive you, argues Ethan McCue in a caustic post sparked by a “T‑Rex giving a thumbs up.” His claim: AI images are a strong negative social signal. Best case, your audience doesn’t mind; common case, they think less of you. No one credits “prompting skill,” and the emotional baggage around AI art hurts your brand.

Alternatives he suggests:
- Quick-and-dirty edit: Grab a photo or public-domain image and crudely mash in what you need (even in MS Paint). It reads as human and is judged more kindly.
- Doodle it: A phone pic of your own sketch—or a child’s drawing—wins warmth and authenticity.
- Commission an artist: Especially if you’re a well-paid software pro, paying a real artist reflects better on you and supports the craft.
- If you’re grifting: AI art can act as a filter to attract the credulous—akin to the old “Nigerian prince” tactic. If that’s your lane, it’s a feature.

Bottom line: In the game theory of signaling, AI art is a reputational risk. Use something obviously human—or pay someone who is.

Here is a summary of the Hacker News discussion for your daily digest, focusing on how the community reacted to Ethan McCue’s anti-AI art stance:

***

### 🗞️ The Daily Digest: The Social Cost of AI Art

**The Spark:** Developer Ethan McCue recently went viral with a spicy take: using AI art is a massive reputational risk. He argues that passing off AI imagery as your own signals "social illiteracy" and damages your brand. His advice? Doodle it yourself, collage real photos, or pay a human artist. If you must use AI, realize it’s acting as a filter for the gullible. 

**The Hacker News Reaction:**
The HN community had a massive, polarized reaction to McCue's premise. While many agreed with the spirit of the post, others felt McCue was ignoring the economic realities of small-scale publishing and indie development. 

Here are the central themes from the debate:

*   **The "AI Slop" Fatigue is Real:** Many users agreed that readers are getting exhausted by what is now universally being dubbed "slop." Some noted that if they see a generic AI-generated "hero image" at the top of a blog post, they immediately assume the text itself was written by ChatGPT and click away.
*   **The Modern Clip Art?** A prominent counter-argument is that AI art is simply the 2024 equivalent of 90s CD-ROM clip art. 25 years ago, people complained about generic vector graphics; today, it’s AI. Some argued that this phase will pass as artists learn to wield AI as a background tool (like Photoshop's smart select) rather than a wholesale generation engine.
*   **The Indie Economics Reality Check:** Several indie app developers and hobbyists pushed back hard on McCue's advice to "just pay an artist." One developer noted that when making a 99-cent iOS game that might only gross $100 total, paying an illustrator $1,000 to $2,000 is financially impossible. For them, AI isn't about "grifting"; it's the only way their hobby projects can have art at all. 
*   **Elitism vs. Taste:** The debate fractured along class and "extremely online" lines. Some users argued that hating AI art is a niche, elitist internet take, pointing out that local, working-class businesses (like neighborhood sushi buffets) use AI for their signs and menus because it's cheap and accessible. Conversely, others argued that having bad taste isn't an elite framing—AI art genuinely gives off the cheesy vibe of "grandma's forwarded chain letters."
*   **The "Harrison Bergeron" Dystopia:** One of the more philosophical threads compared the current AI trend to Kurt Vonnegut’s *Harrison Bergeron*. Critics expressed existential dread that society is willingly flooding itself with mathematically mediocre, machine-generated content, effectively drowning out extraordinary, intentional human expression. 

**The Takeaway:**
If you're publishing a tech blog, the HN consensus leans toward McCue's side: an AI hero image is a negative signal to a savvy audience. However, for indie developers and local businesses operating on zero-dollar budgets, the practical utility of AI art currently outweighs the reputational risk.
