### Scaling Laws, Carefully

#### [Submission URL](https://lilianweng.github.io/posts/2026-06-24-scaling-laws/) | 14 points | by [tehnub](https://news.ycombinator.com/user?id=tehnub) | [8 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48689744)

This deep dive distills a decade of work on scaling laws—the surprisingly reliable rules-of-thumb that predict how model loss falls as you scale parameters (N), data (D), and compute (C ≈ 6ND). The punchline: on log-log plots, loss tends to trace straight lines, letting you forecast the payoffs of bigger models and larger datasets—and choose the best way to spend a fixed compute budget.

Key ideas
- Predictable power laws: Across tasks and architectures, test loss is well fit by terms like A/N^α + B/D^β + E. Architecture mostly shifts the curve up/down; the slope (exponent) looks like a property of the problem.
- Early theory and empirics: Classic results (Amari ’92) and broad studies (Hestness ’17) show power-law learning curves with three phases: data-starved, power-law, and irreducible-error. Small models plateau when data gets large.
- Joint modeling: Rosenfeld ’20 models loss jointly in N and D, enabling extrapolation from small sweeps to larger runs.
- Kaplan vs. Chinchilla, reconciled: Kaplan et al. (2020) suggested making models larger at fixed compute; DeepMind’s Chinchilla (2022) showed compute-optimal training uses much more data per parameter (rule-of-thumb: train on roughly tens of tokens per parameter), making smaller-but-well-trained models outperform under-trained giants. The apparent conflict comes from different data regimes (data-limited vs. data-rich).
- How to fit scaling laws in practice:
  1) Fix N, sweep D (or vice versa) to get exponents.
  2) IsoFLOP profiles: move along curves with constant ND to find the best N–D trade-off at fixed compute.
  3) Parametric fits: learn a small set of coefficients to predict loss across N and D.
- Why power laws? Intuitions point to spectral properties of data/gradients, bias–variance trade-offs, and aggregation over many weakly dependent factors.
- Caveats: Fits can be brittle—data dedup, tokenizer changes, optimizer tweaks, or shifting evaluation metrics move the curves. Regime changes (e.g., leaving the power-law region) can break extrapolations.

Why it matters
- Planning: A handful of small runs can forecast token budgets, model sizes, and expected loss for much larger training jobs.
- Efficiency: IsoFLOP analysis helps allocate compute between parameters and data to avoid training models that are too big for the available tokens.
- Reliability: Scaling laws remain one of the most actionable tools in modern DL—use them as guardrails, but watch for regime shifts and recipe drift.
