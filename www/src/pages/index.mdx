### Making deep learning go brrrr from first principles (2022)

#### [Submission URL](https://horace.io/brrr_intro.html) | 181 points | by [tosh](https://news.ycombinator.com/user?id=tosh) | [63 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48246889)

Summary:
Instead of cargo-culting performance “tips,” this piece builds a first-principles mental model for speeding up deep learning: every training step’s time falls into one of three buckets—compute, memory bandwidth, or overhead. Knowing which regime you’re in dictates which optimizations can actually help. Modern accelerators are built to go fast on big matrix multiplies, but FLOPs have grown faster than memory bandwidth, so much wall time is lost moving data rather than doing math. Non-matmul ops (norms, activations) are a rounding error in FLOPs yet often dominate latency because they’re bandwidth-bound. The article uses a “factory” analogy—overhead sends instructions, bandwidth ships materials, compute does the work—to explain why maximizing compute utilization requires shrinking the other two.

Key points:
- Three regimes to diagnose:
  - Compute-bound: Big, efficient matmuls dominate. You benefit from tensor cores and higher arithmetic intensity.
  - Memory-bandwidth-bound: Time is spent shuttling tensors, especially for pointwise/normalization ops; these can dwarf their tiny FLOP counts.
  - Overhead-bound: Python, kernel launches, and framework glue time dominate when workloads decompose into many small ops.
- Why chase compute: You can trim overhead and bandwidth costs, but you can’t reduce required math without changing the algorithm. Meanwhile, hardware FLOPs are outpacing bandwidth growth, making utilization harder.
- Specialization matters: GPUs (and TPUs even more) are optimized for matmuls. If you’re not hitting tensor cores, you’ll see a fraction of peak FLOPs.
- Practical implication: Treat performance like model tuning—first identify your regime, then apply targeted fixes (e.g., fuse ops and reduce reads/writes for bandwidth-bound cases; batch and vectorize to reduce overhead; use mixed precision and big GEMMs to stay compute-bound).

Why it matters:
This reframes “make it faster” from alchemy to diagnosis. With a simple taxonomy—compute, bandwidth, overhead—you can stop chasing micro-optimizations that don’t touch your bottleneck and focus on changes that actually keep the GPU going “brrrr.”

What’s new
- CODA is a GPU kernel abstraction that rewrites the “everything-around-the-matmul” parts of Transformer blocks—normalization, activations, residual adds, reductions—into epilogues that run while GEMM tiles are still on chip.
- Instead of launching separate, memory-bound kernels that shuttle big tensors to/from HBM, CODA fixes the GEMM mainloop and exposes a small set of composable epilogue primitives (scaling, reductions, pairwise transforms, accumulation).

Why it matters
- In modern training stacks, dense matmuls are fast; the surrounding ops are often the bottleneck due to memory traffic and kernel launch overheads.
- Executing these ops before the GEMM output is written to global memory cuts data movement and preserves the performance characteristics of expert-tuned GEMMs.

How it works
- Express non-attention parts of a Transformer block (forward and backward) as “GEMM + epilogue” programs.
- The constrained interface is simple enough that both humans and LLMs can author high-performance kernels, suggesting an ergonomic path to fuse common patterns like norm → activation → residual add directly after a linear layer.

Takeaway
- CODA generalizes the kernel-fusion playbook: keep data on chip and do more work in the GEMM epilogue. The authors report strong performance across representative Transformer workloads, hinting at a practical bridge between framework-level productivity and hardware-level efficiency.
