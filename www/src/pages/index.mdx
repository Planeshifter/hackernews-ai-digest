What’s new

Why it matters
- Deterministic, hardware‑friendly ML with tiny latency and strong energy efficiency.
- Supports both inference and real‑time updates, opening doors for control, HFT, scientific triggers, and other “react in nanoseconds” use cases.

Takeaway
By quantizing KANs and compiling their 1D spline pieces into LUTs, the work delivers a practical recipe for ultrafast inference and on‑FPGA online learning—leveraging FPGAs’ strengths while avoiding the usual LUT scaling pitfalls.

- What happened:
  - Jacksonville Sheriff’s Office used facial recognition on surveillance footage and a fake Georgia ID, producing an 85% “match” to Richardson.
  - A victim then picked Richardson from a lineup; a warrant was issued and he was arrested in Charlotte.
  - He spent ~1 month in NC jail, then nearly two months in a Florida jail before his attorney’s work records showed he was at his job in North Carolina when the car was sold (he says he’s never been to Florida).
  - Prosecutors dropped the case a year after the investigation began.
  - Richardson alleges racial profiling; he says he was described as “a guy with dreads and a big nose” and placed in a lineup with people who “look nothing like me.”
  - He lost his job, his home, and custody of two children; his online mugshot is still hurting job prospects.

- Official response: JSO says facial recognition was “one tool among many” contributing to probable cause.

- Why it matters:
  - Highlights the real-world harm from overreliance on AI face recognition and fallible eyewitness IDs.
  - Underscores the need for strict policies: FR results as leads only, mandatory corroboration, audit trails, human validation, and robust alibi checks before warrants and extradition.
  - Raises equity concerns around misidentification and lineup practices.
  - Post-exoneration harms (mugshot persistence) show how hard it is to unwind AI-driven errors once they propagate online.

- The human impact: “It’s very traumatizing... I lost everything,” Richardson said. He’s seeking help and resources to rebuild.

**Hacker News Daily Digest: The Devastating Cost of AI-Driven False Positives**

The Hacker News community reacted with deep frustration and outrage to the story of Jalil Richardson, a man wrongfully jailed for months due to a flawed AI facial recognition match and a questionable eyewitness lineup. The discussion quickly moved beyond the technology itself, evolving into a sharp critique of systemic justice failures, statistical illiteracy among law enforcement, and the lack of accountability for prosecutors. 

Here are the central themes from the discussion:

**1. The Statistical Fallacy of "85% Accuracy"**
A major technical talking point was the police department's reliance on an 85% AI "match." Commenters extensively discussed the base-rate fallacy behind this metric. Users pointed out that running an 85%-accurate tool against a database of millions of faces practically guarantees a massive number of false positives. As one user noted, using this technology on a massive scale is essentially "likeness-fishing," and finding a resemblance in a database of 350 million people should never cross the threshold of probable cause.

**2. A Cascade of Human and Systemic Failures**
While AI initiated the error, many commenters argued the true culprit was profound human negligence. Users criticized the "confirmation bias" pipeline: an AI flags a suspect, police put them in a highly suggestive, racially biased photo lineup ("a guy with dreads and a big nose"), and a flawed eyewitness identification seals the warrant. Several commenters pointed out that human eyewitness testimony is notoriously unreliable, comparing the AI/lineup combo to other historically abused policing tools like lie detectors, field sobriety tests, and drug-sniffing dogs. 

**3. "The Process is the Punishment"**
One of the most horrifying aspects of the story to the HN community was the glacial pace of the justice system. Users expressed sheer disbelief that it took months for prosecutors to verify a simple, rock-solid alibi (work timesheets proving Richardson was 400 miles away). Commenters lamented the lack of social safety nets for the wrongfully accused: Richardson lost his job, his home, and custody of his children while sitting in jail presumed innocent. Furthermore, the permanence of online mugshots serves as a lasting, extrajudicial punishment.

**4. The Debate Over Liability and Immunity**
Who is to blame? A debate emerged regarding liability. While some questioned if the AI model creators or software vendors should bear legal responsibility, most laid the blame squarely on law enforcement and the prosecution. There was widespread anger over "Qualified Immunity" and "Prosecutorial Immunity," with several users arguing that ignoring blatant exonerating evidence for months should result in kidnapping or false imprisonment charges for state officials. However, a cynical consensus remained that the system will likely protect its own.

**The Takeaway:**
Ultimately, the HN community viewed this case as a grim warning. The fusion of nascent, statistically misunderstood AI tools with an inflexible, slow-moving judicial system has created a reality where citizens can easily have their lives destroyed by a false-positive algorithm—with little to no recourse for restitution.
