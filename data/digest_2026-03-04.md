### You need to rewrite your CLI for AI agents

#### [Submission URL](https://justin.poehnelt.com/posts/rewrite-your-cli-for-ai-agents/) | 141 points | by [justinwp](https://news.ycombinator.com/user?id=justinwp) | [62 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47252459)

TL;DR: Design CLIs “agents-first.” Treat LLMs as your primary users: accept raw API payloads, expose self-describing schemas at runtime, stream minimal, deterministic JSON, and aggressively harden inputs to defend against hallucinations—while still keeping a human-friendly path in the same binary.

Highlights
- Human DX vs Agent DX: People want discoverability and forgiveness; agents need predictability and defense-in-depth. Trying to retrofit a human-first CLI for agents is a losing bet.
- Raw JSON over bespoke flags: Prefer a single --json or --params that takes the full API payload 1:1 with your backend schema. Keep convenience flags for humans, but make the raw path first-class.
- Dual-mode without two tools: Support OUTPUT_FORMAT or --output json and emit NDJSON when stdout isn’t a TTY so agents get machine-readable, streaming output.
- Schema introspection instead of docs: Provide a schema command that returns method signatures, params, request/response types, and scopes as machine-readable JSON at runtime. No stale, token-heavy docs in prompts.
- Context-window discipline: Enforce field masks so agents fetch only what they need, and use NDJSON pagination to stream-process results without loading giant arrays into context.
- Input hardening for hallucinations: Treat the CLI as the last line of defense. Sandbox file outputs, reject control characters, and validate resource IDs to block query params or double-encoded strings (%), preventing traversal and encoding pitfalls that agents commonly generate.

Why it matters: CLIs are the lowest-friction bridge from agents to real systems. Making output deterministic and inputs robust reduces token costs, boosts reliability, and prevents subtle, high-impact errors. Poehnelt’s “gws” (Google Workspace) CLI embodies these patterns using Google Discovery Docs for live schema and strict validation at the edges.

Also in the post (by section headings): Ship agent skills, not just commands; multi-surface support (MCP, extensions, env vars); safety rails like dry-run and response sanitization; and where to start.

### Speculative Speculative Decoding (SSD)

#### [Submission URL](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.03251) | 60 points | by [E-Reverance](https://news.ycombinator.com/user?id=E-Reverance) | [9 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47242637)

Speculative decoding lets a small “draft” model guess ahead while a larger “target” model verifies, but it still has a serial bottleneck: you wait for verification before drafting the next chunk. This paper proposes Speculative Speculative Decoding (SSD), which keeps the draft model busy during verification by predicting likely verification outcomes and prebuilding speculations for them. If the verifier’s result lands in that predicted set, the system can return tokens immediately—skipping the usual drafting stall.

They package the approach as Saguaro, outline the key challenges SSD introduces, and give principled fixes. Reported speeds: up to 2x faster than optimized speculative decoding baselines and up to 5x faster than plain autoregressive decoding on open-source inference engines. Net effect: less idle time in the draft–verify loop and lower end-to-end latency without changing the single-pass verification step.

### LLMs can unmask pseudonymous users at scale with surprising accuracy

#### [Submission URL](https://arstechnica.com/security/2026/03/llms-can-unmask-pseudonymous-users-at-scale-with-surprising-accuracy/) | 91 points | by [Gagarin1917](https://news.ycombinator.com/user?id=Gagarin1917) | [67 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47242453)

AI can now de‑anonymize “burner” social accounts with surprising accuracy, researchers say, undermining the long-held assumption that pseudonyms provide practical privacy online.

What’s new
- Researchers built LLM-driven agents that extract identity clues from free text, then browse the web to find and verify likely real identities—no neatly structured datasets needed.
- Across tests, recall (how many users were correctly deanonymized) reached up to 68%, with precision (how often a guess was right) up to 90%, far exceeding classical deanonymization approaches modeled after the “Netflix Prize” attack.

How they tested it
- Cross-platform posts: Collected linked Hacker News and LinkedIn profiles, stripped obvious identifiers from posts, and let an LLM match texts back to people.
- Anonymized “micro-identity” data: Revisited the Netflix-style setting to compare against older methods.
- Reddit histories: Split a single user’s Reddit activity and asked the model to match halves; also looked at r/movies plus niche film subs to see how topic overlap affects identifiability.
- Interview-style data: From an Anthropic questionnaire, the agent identified 7% of 125 participants using only general life details.

By the numbers
- General results: up to 68% recall; up to 90% precision.
- r/movies experiment:
  - 1 shared movie: 3.1% of users identified at 90% precision (1.2% at 99%).
  - 5–9 shared movies: 8.4% at 90% precision (2.5% at 99%).
  - 10+ shared movies: 48.1% at 90% precision (17% at 99%).
- Even the 7% recall from lightweight questionnaire answers is notable—it shows end-to-end deanonymization from free text is now feasible.

Why it matters
- Pseudonymity’s threat model is broken: targeted deanonymization no longer requires extensive manual effort.
- Risks include doxxing, stalking, and granular marketing profiles that tie interests and habits to real identities across platforms.

Caveats and trajectory
- Some scenarios still show modest recall, but the capability exists and is improving quickly as LLMs get better at reasoning, web interaction, and verification.
- The research used public data and altered details to protect subjects; real-world performance will vary by data quality and user behavior.

Takeaways
- Assume that consistent writing style, niche interests, workplace or location hints, and cross-posting patterns can link accounts.
- Expect platforms, researchers, and policymakers to explore defenses (rate limits, redaction tools, privacy by default) and to revisit norms around pseudonymous participation.
