#### [Submission URL](https://deepswe.datacurve.ai/blog) | 59 points | by [ammar_x](https://news.ycombinator.com/user?id=ammar_x) | [17 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48284939)

DeepSWE: a contamination‑free, long‑horizon benchmark for coding agents

What it is
- A new software engineering benchmark designed to feel like real agent work: short, behavior‑focused prompts that require agents to explore large codebases and implement substantial changes.
- Authors say it delivers four advances over common public benchmarks: contamination‑free tasks, broad repository coverage, higher real‑world complexity, and behavior‑based verification.

Why it’s different
- Contamination control: Every task’s reference solution is written from scratch and never merged upstream, reducing the chance models saw the answer during pretraining.
- Realistic workload: Prompts are roughly half as long as SWE‑Bench Pro’s, but solutions require 5.5x more code and about 2x more output tokens.
- Broader scope: 113 tasks across 91 active repos in 5 languages (TypeScript, Go, Python, JavaScript, Rust), aiming to reflect the heterogeneity developers face.
- Verification focus: Handwritten verifiers check behavior, not specific implementations. The authors’ audit of SWE‑Bench Pro found 8% false positives and 24% false negatives in grading, plus evidence of contamination risk.

By the numbers (vs. SWE‑Bench)
- Mean prompt length (chars): DeepSWE 2,158; Pro 4,614; Verified 1,700
- Mean lines added: DeepSWE 668; Pro 120; Verified 10
- Mean files edited: DeepSWE 7; Pro 5; Verified 1

Leaderboard snapshot (mini-swe-agent harness; authors’ report)
- gpt‑5.5 [xhigh]: 70% ±4
- gpt‑5.4 [xhigh]: 56% ±5
- claude‑opus‑4.7 [max]: 54% ±5
- claude‑sonnet‑4.6 [high]: 32% ±4
- gemini‑3.5‑flash [medium]: 28% ±4
… tailing to single digits for several models

Why it matters
- The authors argue DeepSWE better separates frontier agents in ways practitioners recognize from day‑to‑day workflows. All runs are reproducible, with code, trajectories, and harness details available to inspect or to run your own agent.

### A sleep-like consolidation mechanism for LLMs

#### [Submission URL](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.26099) | 207 points | by [juxtapose](https://news.ycombinator.com/user?id=juxtapose) | [135 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48281226)

Language Models Need Sleep (arXiv:2605.26099)

TL;DR: The authors propose a “sleep” phase for long-context LLMs that periodically consolidates recent context into persistent fast weights, clears the KV cache, and shifts extra compute to offline passes—improving long-horizon and deeper reasoning without increasing per-token latency during normal inference.

Key ideas
- Problem: Attention scales poorly with context length; KV caches get unwieldy on long-horizon tasks.
- Approach: Insert sleep-like consolidation steps. The model runs N offline recurrent passes over the accumulated context and updates state-space model (SSM) blocks via a learned local rule, producing persistent fast weights. Afterward, it clears the KV cache.
- Runtime effect: Regular “wake” predictions keep low latency; extra computation happens during scheduled sleep intervals.

Results
- On controlled synthetic tasks (cellular automata, multi-hop graph retrieval) and a realistic math reasoning benchmark, the sleep mechanism succeeds where standard Transformers and SSM–attention hybrids fail.
- Longer sleep duration (larger N) yields better performance, with the biggest gains on examples requiring deeper reasoning.

Why it matters
- Offers a practical path to longer effective context and multi-step reasoning without quadratic attention costs at inference time.
- Bridges ideas from consolidation/fast weights and modern SSM blocks, suggesting a reusable memory that survives past context window limits.

Caveats and open questions
- Overheads shift to sleep phases; optimal scheduling and stability of fast-weight updates need exploration.
- Evidence so far is synthetic tasks plus one realistic math setting—generalization to diverse, real-world long-context workloads remains to be shown.

Paper: https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.26099 (PDF available)

### 🧠 HN Digest: Do LLMs Need a Nap? 

Today’s top discussion on Hacker News centers around a fascinating new paper proposing a "sleep" phase for Large Language Models. To solve the problem of unwieldy, memory-hogging KV caches during long-horizon tasks, researchers suggest letting the model periodically go "offline" to consolidate recent contexts into persistent fast weights, clear its cache, and wake up refreshed. 

Here’s what the Hacker News community had to say about the implications, the tech, and the terminology:

**1. "Sleep" vs. Anthropomorphism**
A significant portion of the thread debated the paper's biological framing. Several commenters strongly disliked the anthropomorphizing of background GPU compute, pointing out that "computers don't sleep," and that globally distributed networks don't actually have "offline hours." 
However, others pushed back, noting that the biological analogy is actually highly mechanically accurate here. Just as biological sleep uses hippocampal replay to transfer short-term memories into long-term synaptic weights, this architecture halts external inputs to recursively compute and consolidate short-term context (the KV cache) into persistent weights. As one user put it: without periodically cleaning and consolidating context, an LLM effectively "goes insane."

**2. Clarifying the Technical Mechanics**
Some users initially assumed the paper was just talking about basic context pruning or computationally expensive full-weight retraining. More diligent readers stepped in to clarify the mechanics:
*   **Not full retraining:** Updating weights based on a 10k to 1M token context window is computationally trivial compared to training a baseline model on 10 trillion tokens. 
*   **State-Space Update:** The method specifically updates localized "fast weights" or state-space model (SSM/Mamba) states rather than doing a massive overhaul of the base parameters.
*   Commenters likened the approach to recent Continuous Learning frameworks and "E2E-TTT" (End-to-End Test-Time Training), praising it as an elegant way for models to adapt to unseen data distributions on the fly.

**3. Homebrew Implementations & Preventing "Collapse"**
One highly upvoted comment revealed they had already built a DIY version of this concept months ago. To handle KV caches nearing capacity, they created a three-tier memory system:
*   **Short-term memory:** The standard KV cache.
*   **Mid-term memory:** "Replay buffers" aggregated offline.
*   **Long-term memory:** The base model weights. 
By pulling the accumulated data and running an offline LoRA fine-tune for a few hours, they achieved similar results. Interestingly, this user noted that to prevent *model collapse* (catastrophic forgetting or hallucination loops), they had to mix this new mid-term data with baseline "anchor data" from the original training set. They likened this data-mixing process to the biological function of *dreaming*.

**4. Parallels in Recent Research**
The community pointed out that effectively leveraging "offline" or background compute is quickly becoming a major trend. Users linked to a recent preprint on "Scaling Test-Time Compute," which showed that giving models offline time to anticipate queries and pre-compute/think dramatically reduces inference costs and boosts accuracy (up to 18% on benchmarks like AIME). 

**The Takeaway:**
While the "sleep" terminology might rub rigid technologists the wrong way, the consensus on HN is that moving from stateless, ever-expanding KV caches to dynamic, multi-tiered memory consolidation is the next logical step toward more persistent, agentic, and capable AI. 

### Eagle 3.1: Collaboration Between the EAGLE Team, vLLM Team, and TorchSpec Team

#### [Submission URL](https://vllm.ai/blog/2026-05-26-eagle-3-1) | 66 points | by [berlianta](https://news.ycombinator.com/user?id=berlianta) | [22 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48278407)

EAGLE 3.1: a sturdier, faster take on speculative decoding lands in vLLM

- What’s new: The teams behind EAGLE, vLLM, and TorchSpec released EAGLE 3.1, a major update to the widely used EAGLE speculative decoding family. It targets a key fragility they call “attention drift,” where deeper speculation causes the drafter to focus on its own tokens instead of the input “sink” tokens, degrading acceptance rates in the wild.

- Why it was breaking: Two issues stacked up at higher speculation depths:
  - Imbalanced fused inputs as higher-layer hidden states dominate the drafter.
  - Hidden-state magnitude growth via the unnormalized residual path.

- The fix: EAGLE 3.1 introduces:
  - FC normalization after each target hidden state (pre-FC).
  - Feeding post-norm hidden states into subsequent decoding steps.
  Together, this makes multi-step drafting behave more like repeatedly calling the drafter, improving stability across contexts.

- Results:
  - Better training-to-inference generalization.
  - Stronger long-context robustness and resilience to chat template/system prompt changes.
  - More stable acceptance lengths; up to 2× longer acceptance in long-context workloads vs. EAGLE 3.

- Real-world integration:
  - vLLM: EAGLE 3.1 is a config-driven extension of EAGLE 3 with backward compatibility, so operators can drop in new draft models without changing the serving path. Support is in vLLM main, nightly, and coming in v0.22.0.
  - TorchSpec: Adds efficient training support for EAGLE 3.1, lowering iteration overhead.

- Open model + benchmarks:
  - Released a draft model for Kimi K2.6 on Hugging Face (lightseekorg/kimi-k2.6-eagle3.1-mla).
  - On Kimi-K2.6-NVFP4 with vLLM (TP=4, GB200, non-disaggregated) on SPEED-Bench coding:
    - 2.03× higher per-user output throughput at concurrency 1.
    - Still strong at scale: 1.71× (C=4), 1.66× (C=16).

- Big picture: A clean diagnosis (attention drift) plus lightweight architectural tweaks (FC norm + post-norm feedback) yields a more robust, production-friendly speculative decoding path. With seamless vLLM integration and TorchSpec training, this is a practical speedup that should translate to higher throughput and steadier acceptance across diverse prompts and long contexts.

### 1. The "Accuracy vs. Speed" Misconception
A central part of the discussion cleared up how speculative decoding actually affects LLM outputs. User *kbmsk* asked a common question: doesn't speculative decoding hurt model accuracy? 
*   **The Consensus:** Speculative decoding does **not** change the actual text (accuracy) output by the AI. The smaller "draft" model guesses the next tokens, but the large "parent" model always verifies them. 
*   **The Catch:** "Performance" in this context strictly means *speed*. As *ketchup32613* and *dmnlgst* explained, if the draft model is bad at guessing, the rejection rate spikes. In a worst-case scenario, speculative decoding can actually be slower than standard generation because you are wasting compute and memory bandwidth generating and rejecting bad drafted tokens.

### 2. Is it Worth it for Local Hobbyists?
There was a heavy debate over who actually benefits from speculative decoding: cloud providers or local users with hardware constraints.
*   **The Cloud Advantage:** User *gcr* noted that speculative decoding is fantastic for inference providers with massive VRAM (128GB+), but tough for home users on constrained hardware (like a 32GB M1 Mac). Fitting both a main model and a draft model into memory often forces users to heavily quantize the models, which can cause the draft model's guesses to drift from the main model, negating the speedup.
*   **The Low-Concurrency Sweet Spot:** *NitpickLawyer* and *tssg* provided a technical counterpoint. Speculative decoding and standard request "batching" are solving the same bottleneck: memory bandwidth. Therefore, speculative decoding is highly effective for single-user, unbatched local inference (low concurrency). However, as you scale up to enterprise-level high concurrency, traditional batching takes over and the benefits of speculative decoding diminish. 

### 3. The "PCB Design" Naming Collision

### The Open/Closed Problem in AI

#### [Submission URL](https://blog.mempko.com/the-open-closed-problem-in-ai/) | 14 points | by [vinhnx](https://news.ycombinator.com/user?id=vinhnx) | [5 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48276432)

MLSys: Are we hardening the wrong AI paradigm?
- After attending MLSys, the author argues the field is optimizing itself into a corner: nearly all effort targets efficiency for today’s open-loop LLMs (train offline, deploy frozen), not models that learn continuously on their own.
- He draws a hardware analogy: CPUs (open) → fixed-function GPUs (closed) → programmable GPUs/CUDA (open) → today’s specialized training/inference ASICs (closing again). Inference chips literally assume frozen weights—read-optimized memory, separated compute/memory, big batched matmuls—making on-the-fly learning an afterthought at the silicon level.
- He contrasts open-loop AI (external trainer, static params; “agents” hack learning via external tools) with brains’ closed-loop learning (model updates itself from prediction errors). The claim: each new efficiency win hardens infrastructure around the wrong loop.
- What’s missing: a substrate for self-updating models—fine-grain, fused compute+memory where parameters can rewrite themselves in place (think “FPGA-like, but for learning”).
- Provocation: If you’re only chasing open-loop efficiency, you’re making the breakthrough—closed-loop learning—harder to find. Build the experimental hardware before specialization cements the status quo.

Here is a summary of the Hacker News discussion regarding the article on whether we are hardening the wrong AI paradigm:

**Defense of the Current "Open-Loop" Paradigm**
Several commenters pushed back against the author's premise, arguing that the current architecture—using static models augmented by external tools, prompts, and agent harnesses—is actually highly beneficial for developers. Storing data and logic outside of opaque neural weights makes systems much more accessible, mechanistic, and interpretable. It allows ordinary software engineers (not just AI researchers) to read, modify, and build upon these systems using standard programming languages and English prompts. Rather than viewing "agent harnesses" as a hack, users expect a lot of future innovation in this space, potentially leading to systems where harnesses train smaller, highly-scoped models for specific tasks. 

**Economic Realities and Hardware Inertia**
Other users agreed with the author's observation about hardware polarization, but attributed it to basic market economics. Optimization money flows toward making proven, profitable paradigms faster, rather than risking capital on unproven hardware architectures. The sheer scale of the inertia was highlighted, with one user noting that massive corporate capital expenditure—projected by analysts like Gartner to reach hundreds of billions globally by 2026—is already locked into building data centers, GPUs, and ASICs optimized for the current ecosystem. 

**Secretive Research**
Finally, at least one commenter challenged the idea that continuous learning is being ignored by the broader field, suggesting instead that continuous fine-tuning and closed-loop systems are being heavily researched behind closed doors. According to this view, top AI labs are simply keeping their cards close to their chest while the massive public-facing infrastructure is built out.
