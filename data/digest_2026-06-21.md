Teach Me Cool Stuff: Fine-tuning a tiny local LLM to categorize questions

- The project: Build a household chatbot that uses RAG, but first routes each question into a metadata category (pool, car, hvac, cooking, etc.) to narrow the vector search and improve retrieval.
- Setup: Qwen 3 4B handles general answers; a super-tiny Qwen 3 0.6B acts as the category classifier. The experiment asks: can a 600M-parameter model be fine-tuned into a reliable, single-label classifier?

- Why it matters: A tiny, local, fine-tuned classifier can be a fast, private, and cheap front-end for RAG pipelines—routing queries to the right slice of your index. Pattern: small classifier for routing + slightly larger model for answering.

### When I reject AI code even if it works

#### [Submission URL](https://vinibrasil.com/when-i-reject-ai-code-even-if-it-works/) | 226 points | by [vnbrs](https://news.ycombinator.com/user?id=vnbrs) | [165 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48614631)

Title: The real bottleneck with AI coding agents isn’t implementation—it’s review

Summary:
A developer argues that while coding agents can generate code quickly, the hard part has shifted to reviewing large diffs you didn’t mentally model yourself. Even with planning, phasing, and small PRs, the cognitive load of validating AI output is high. The author often rejects agent-generated changes on a second pass—not because the model changes, but because their own understanding does. Passing tests and green CI aren’t enough; good engineering still requires fit, clarity, and maintainability, which demand human judgment.

Rules of thumb for rejecting AI-generated code:
- If you can’t explain the approach in your own words.
- If the diff is bigger than the problem.
- If it introduces abstractions before they’re proven necessary.
- If it “works locally” but makes the system harder to reason about.
- If you find yourself trusting the output more than your understanding.

Why it matters:
- Agents accelerate implementation, not comprehension; review becomes the choke point.
- Required human review should accompany AI review to prevent expedient but poor designs.
- Agents are powerful copilots, but still need a strong engineer to drive toward sustainable solutions.

**Hacker News Daily Digest: AI Coding Agents and the Review Bottleneck**

In today’s top story, a developer sparked a massive discussion by arguing that the true bottleneck of AI coding agents is no longer generating code—it’s the exhausting cognitive load of reviewing it. **The "Scary Middle" and PR Spam**
Many commenters expressed deep concern over what they call the "scary middle": coworkers—and job-seeking "NPCs" on public GitHub repos—using AI to rapidly churn out code just to close tickets and collect credit. Because these developers aren't deeply verifying the AI's output, it falls on reviewers to catch poorly designed code. **The Over-complication Trap**
A recurring complaint is that LLMs (with Claude being specifically named) tend to over-engineer solutions. Instead of adhering to the KISS (Keep It Simple, Stupid) principle, agents frequently introduce unnecessary, complex enterprise-level abstractions and design patterns. Developers with backgrounds in streamlined frameworks (like Ruby on Rails or Clojure) find themselves fighting the AI's tendency to write highly convoluted code. A popular workaround shared by users is explicitly prompting the AI to "make it simpler" across 2-3 iterations.

**Sycophancy and The Illusion of Reasoning**
Users voiced frustration over AI "sycophancy." When developers ask clarifying questions about a piece of code, the AI often misinterprets the inquiry as pushback. It will immediately apologize ("You're absolutely right!"), abandon perfectly good solutions, and confidently substitute them with incorrect assumptions. Commenters pointed out that AI flattery acts as a distraction from its fragility; the model isn't actually "reasoning," but rather generating plausible-sounding, confident text that mimics logical processes. 

**Sneaky Errors and the Limitations of Trust**
In highly complex or specialized domains—such as Machine Learning implementations—AIs are prone to making subtle, silent errors. If the developer lacks deep domain experience, they are highly unlikely to catch these mistakes. Even when unit tests pass, end-to-end (E2E) tests will fail in bizarre ways. Reviewers note that when you try to fix these issues with the AI, you quickly realize it cannot hold the entire mental model of the system. Ultimately, developers are forced to rip out the AI’s complex, commented-up code and rewrite it from scratch.
