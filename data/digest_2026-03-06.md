### A tool that removes censorship from open-weight LLMs

#### [Submission URL](https://github.com/elder-plinius/OBLITERATUS) | 191 points | by [mvdwoord](https://news.ycombinator.com/user?id=mvdwoord) | [82 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47275291)

OBLITERATUS: open-source toolkit to “liberate” LLMs from refusals

- What it is: A mechanistic-interpretability toolkit that locates and removes a model’s internal “refusal” directions without retraining. It targets the subspaces that drive safety refusals and projects them out at inference time, aiming to preserve core language ability while increasing willingness to respond. Ships with a Gradio UI on Hugging Face Spaces (ZeroGPU-friendly) and a Python API. AGPL-3.0 licensed. Repo sits at ~2.1k stars, 371 forks.

- How it works: 
  - Probes hidden states on restricted vs. unrestricted prompts.
  - Extracts refusal directions using methods like SVD, PCA, mean-difference, sparse autoencoders, and whitened SVD.
  - Performs norm-preserving projection/steering to remove those directions, then runs perplexity/coherence checks.
  - Includes ablation studies across layers/heads/FFNs and 15+ analysis modules to map where “guardrails” live and how entangled they are with capabilities.
  - An “informed” mode auto-configures choices (which layers, how many directions, etc.) based on on-the-fly analysis.

- Community-powered research: Optional telemetry crowdsources anonymous benchmarks on model architectures, refusal geometry, and method performance to inform future techniques.

- Why it matters: 
  - Technically: Offers a reproducible pipeline for isolating and intervening on safety-related circuits, advancing transparency in how refusals are implemented inside transformers.
  - Practically: Enables fine-grained control over model behavior without fine-tuning, with an easy UI and no-code path.
  - Controversially: Raises clear safety and ethics questions about dismantling guardrails, and will likely spark debate over responsible release, use, and evaluation.

**Skepticism regarding technical implementation:**
Information security and AI researchers in the comments distinguish between legitimate "refusal ablation" (often called "abliteration"—using SVD to identify and orthogonalize refusal vectors) and what they perceive this toolkit is doing. Critics argue the project employs "dumb ablation"—simply zeroing out large components or layers—which implies a fundamental misunderstanding of Transformer architecture and linear algebra. They warn that "novel" methods listed in the repository likely amount to heuristic search strategies that break model capabilities rather than surgically removing refusals.

**Documentation and credibility concerns:**
Multiple users criticize the repository’s README, describing it as "AI-generated pseudo-science" filled with jargon that obscures the actual methodology. The author’s reputation (`elder_plinius`) is a point of contention; while some acknowledge his prominence in the jailbreaking scene, others point to his promotion of questionable hardware (such as a $900 "microphone jamming" device) as evidence of "snake oil" salesmanship.

**Theoretical debate on guardrails:**
The discussion touches on the nature of model safety, with commenters suggesting that in advanced models (like Gemini), refusal behaviors are deeply entangled with general reasoning capabilities. Consequently, users are skeptical that a toolkit can simply "switch off" refusals without effectively lobotomizing the model's intelligence. While a few users claim success with the tool, the prevailing sentiment frames it as "AI slop" or hype rather than a breakthrough in mechanistic interpretability.

### What if AI just makes us work harder?

#### [Submission URL](https://www.ft.com/content/e8bb5ab1-4b4d-473e-8f76-e690443e9fb4) | 45 points | by [paulpauper](https://news.ycombinator.com/user?id=paulpauper) | [12 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47281443)

- Core idea: Instead of delivering leisure and shorter weeks, AI’s productivity boosts may be captured by employers as higher targets, faster turnaround times, tighter monitoring, and “always-on” expectations—intensifying work rather than reducing it.
- Historical rhyme: Past tech (email, spreadsheets, algorithmic scheduling) sped tasks but also raised the bar—more messages to answer, more metrics to hit, less slack time. AI could be a force-multiplier for this dynamic.
- Where it bites: Knowledge work (more drafts, more content to review), customer service and logistics (AI-driven monitoring/scripting), and creative roles (baseline output gets commoditized, raising pressure to differentiate).
- The paradox: Early studies show AI often boosts junior/average worker output and quality, but that doesn’t automatically translate into fewer hours or less stress—often it resets expectations upward.
- What would change the outcome: Intentional choices—right-to-disconnect policies, four-day-week experiments, limits on surveillance, and incentive structures that share productivity gains as time off or higher pay—rather than assuming technology alone yields more leisure.

Why it matters: If AI amplifies pace and oversight without redesigning jobs, we risk a future with more output and less wellbeing—an outcome driven more by management and policy than by the tech itself.

HN angles likely to spark debate:
- Will firms use AI to cut drudgery or to ratchet KPIs?
- Can team-level norms (no after-hours pings, meeting caps) offset the acceleration pressure?
- Measuring “productivity” vs. value: are we just creating more to read/watch/respond to?
- What evidence would show actual work-time reductions from AI—and who captures the surplus?

Here is a summary of the discussion:

**The "Joy of Drudgery" and Mental Exhaustion**
A dominant theme in the comments is that AI tools automate the "easy" parts of work (like writing boilerplate code), which paradoxically makes the workday harder. Several users argue that low-stakes tasks provided necessary mental breaks and "dopamine hits" from quick wins. With those removed, workers are left with 100% "high leverage" activity—specifically reviewing, debugging, and curating AI output—which is described as mentally exhausting, less creative, and far more tedious than the act of creation itself.

**Empirical Evidence of Longer Hours**
One commenter cites a recent Scientific American article (referencing a Multitudes study) suggesting that AI adoption in software development has correlated with longer hours overall. The study purportedly shows a 196% increase in time spent fixing bugs, along with a rise in out-of-hours commits and weekend work, contradicting the narrative of increased leisure.

**The "Friction" Analogy**
A discussion thread compares modern AI-assisted work to playing *Counter-Strike* versus playing Cricket. Cricket requires significant logistical friction (travel, setup, physical limits) which naturally bounds the activity. *Counter-Strike* (like low-friction AI tools) allows for instant, continuous loops of engagement. The argument is that by removing the "friction" of work, AI enables—and perhaps coerces—employees to work for hours on end without the natural pauses that previous workflows necessitated.
