### Attention Residuals

#### [Submission URL](https://github.com/MoonshotAI/Attention-Residuals) | 216 points | by [GaggiX](https://news.ycombinator.com/user?id=GaggiX) | [29 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47458595)

MoonshotAI’s “Attention Residuals” (AttnRes): make residuals attend over depth

- What it is: A drop-in replacement for standard Transformer residual connections. Instead of uniformly adding every previous layer’s output, each layer uses a learned, input-dependent softmax over all earlier representations (attention over depth).
- Why it matters: Uniform accumulation in PreNorm can dilute signal and blow up hidden-state magnitudes as depth increases. AttnRes aims to keep magnitudes bounded and distribute gradients more evenly across layers, improving training stability and efficiency.
- How it works: For layer l, h_l is a weighted sum of all prior outputs, with weights computed from a single learned “pseudo-query” per layer. A practical “Block AttnRes” variant attends over block-level summaries (not every layer) to cut memory from O(Ld) to O(Nd); ~8 blocks reportedly recover most gains with marginal overhead.
- Results:
  - Scaling: Matches the loss of a baseline trained with ~1.25x more compute.
  - Downstream (Kimi Linear 48B / 3B activated, 1.4T tokens): broad improvements, notably GPQA-Diamond +7.5 (36.9 → 44.4) and HumanEval +3.1 (59.1 → 62.2). Also modest lifts on MMLU, BBH, TriviaQA, Math, MBPP, CMMLU, and C-Eval.
- Implementation: PyTorch-style pseudocode provided; apply AttnRes before both attention and MLP; block boundaries handled by simple bookkeeping.

Takeaway: A lightweight architectural tweak that treats depth like a sequence, trading uniform residual sums for content-aware aggregation—and delivering consistent quality and compute-efficiency gains in reported tests.

**The Author:** A significant portion of the discussion focuses on the revelation that the lead author is a high school student. This sparked a geopolitical debate regarding STEM education systems, comparing the output of engineering talent in China, India, and the West, alongside arguments about the reliability of Chinese demographic and enrollment statistics.

**Performance & Efficiency Interpretations:**
*   **Training vs. Inference:** Users debated the paper’s performance claims. While some initially interpreted "1.25x compute efficiency" as faster training or reduced inference bandwidth, others clarified that this metric refers to **training efficiency** (achieving the same loss with less compute).
*   **Inference Overhead:** Commenters noted that inference is strictly simpler (and potentially faster) in standard Transformers; AttnRes introduces slight overhead. The claim of "1/6th memory bandwidth" was debunked by some as a specific comparison to DeepSeek’s mHC architecture, rather than a general improvement for consumer hardware.

**Technical Details:**
*   **Mechanism:** Users compared the architecture to LSTM input gates, noting that it replaces the "uniform addition" of standard residual connections with a mechanism that lets the model "decide" how much of the previous signal to use via attention.
*   **Block AttnRes:** Commenters highlighted the "Block" variant as essential for implementation, explaining that it partitions layers to reduce memory complexity from $O(Ld)$ to $O(Nd)$, making the method practical with only marginal overhead.

### NumKong: 2'000 Mixed Precision Kernels for All

#### [Submission URL](https://ashvardanian.com/posts/numkong/) | 45 points | by [ashvardanian](https://news.ycombinator.com/user?id=ashvardanian) | [4 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47459447)

SimSIMD is reborn as NumKong: a CPU-first, mixed-precision tensor math library with ~2,000 SIMD kernels across 200k lines, spanning C, C++, Rust, Swift, JavaScript, Go, and Python. It aims to bring GPU-era numeric formats and tiled compute to mainstream CPUs, with a strong emphasis on correctness, portability, and developer UX.

What’s new
- Broad ISA coverage: RISC-V Vector (RVV), Intel AMX, and Arm SME tiles, plus classic SIMD backends.
- Mixed-precision everywhere: BF16, FP16, FP8, and even “Float6” (E3M2/E2M3) on any CPU; native Int4/UInt4 dot products via nibble algebra.
- Numerics you can trust: compensated sums (Neumaier, Dot2), Ozaki scheme for Float64 GEMM using Float32 tile hardware, validated against in-house 118‑bit floats.
- End-to-end ops: from GEMM-like batched dots to geospatial (Haversine/Vincenty, claimed up to 5,300x faster than GeoPy), 3D alignment (Kabsch/Umeyama, ~200x over BioPython), and ColBERT-style fused MaxSim for GPU-free late interaction.
- WebAssembly SIMD backend for sandboxes, edge, and browsers.
- Lean delivery: ~5 MB binaries and wheels for 7 languages.

Performance snapshot (single-thread, Intel Xeon4)
- Trades speed for accuracy in FP64/FP32 (e.g., tighter FP64 error ~1e‑16), but shines in low-precision:
  - FP16: approaches PyTorch MKL speed with lower error.
  - FP8 and Int8: very large speedups with “0% err” claims (exact/overflow-safe accumulation), e.g., Int8 ~1,279 gso/s vs 50 gso/s in PyTorch+MKL.
- Much smaller binary size than PyTorch+MKL (5 MB vs ~705 MB shown).

RISC-V state of play (and why NumKong cares)
- No public cloud RVV 1.0 yet; Scaleway’s C910 is stuck at 0.7.1 (binary-incompatible with 1.0, missing key features like fractional LMUL and some BF16 support).
- No ratified RVV matrix extension yet, unlike AMX/SME.
- Still, RVV offers standout instructions NumKong exploits: segment loads (deinterleave AoS), compact/scan (viota+vcompress), widening reductions (vfwredusum), and unique widening FMAs (e.g., f64 += f32×f32 without intermediate rounding), enabling robust, high-precision kernels.

Why it matters
- Pushes CPU-only inference and data processing forward with modern formats and tight error control.
- Portable, small-footprint kernels across seven languages and WASM open up edge and browser scenarios.
- Offers an alternative design point to BLAS/MKL: accuracy-first where it matters, plus aggressive low-precision throughput with correctness guarantees.

Caveats
- Benchmarks are single-threaded on Sapphire Rapids; multi-thread and cross-hardware results will vary.
- RISC-V support is constrained by ecosystem maturity and spec fragmentation.
- Some headline numbers (e.g., “0% err” in FP8/Int8) reflect author-provided tests/settings; broader validation will be of interest to users.

Sam Altman: AI is underhyped, AGI would be the biggest tech shift ever

- The pitch: Despite its spotty history and bad reputation, AI—especially AGI—deserves far more optimism. If it works, it will eclipse every prior tech breakthrough.
- Progress so far: We already have superhuman performance in narrow domains (chess, aviation, etc.), but we tend to downplay these as “not real AI.” Serious efforts exist, often behind closed doors.
- Why optimism: Andrew Ng’s “single learning algorithm” hypothesis suggests one general method could learn across modalities (ears, eyes, etc.). - Possible endgame: Maybe we don’t want conscious machines. A healthy equilibrium could be computers excelling at doing while humans specialize in thinking and creativity—an intuitive division of labor if machine creativity never truly emerges.

Why it matters: If AGI arrives, it’s the biggest technological discontinuity in memory; if it doesn’t, AI still reshapes work by turbocharging execution while keeping human judgment and desire at the center.

### MacBook M5 Pro and Qwen3.5 = Local AI Security System

HomeSec-Bench v1: local 9B model nears GPT-5.4 on domain tasks, fully offline

Aegis-AI released HomeSec-Bench v1, a domain-specific benchmark for AI home security assistants (96 LLM + 35 VLM tests across 16 suites). Headline result: Qwen3.5-9B, running entirely on a MacBook Pro M5 Pro via llama.cpp, scores 93.8%—within 4.1 points of GPT-5.4 (97.9%) and 2 points of GPT-5.4-mini—while beating GPT-5.4-nano by 1 point. The pitch: near–frontier accuracy on real workflows with zero API cost and full data privacy.

Key numbers
- Qwen3.5-9B (local): 93.8% pass, 25 tok/s, 765 ms TTFT, ~13.8 GB memory
- Hardware: MacBook Pro M5 Pro, 18 cores, 64 GB unified memory, macOS 15.3
- TTFT standout: Qwen3.5-35B-MoE averages 435 ms—lower than OpenAI’s GPT-5.4-nano (508 ms), mini (553 ms), and GPT-5.4 (601 ms)
- Decode speeds: cloud models still lead (GPT-5.4-mini 234 tok/s; nano 136 tok/s), while local Qwen tops at 41.9 tok/s (35B-MoE) and 25 tok/s (9B)

What it tests (selected)
- Tool use and parameter selection
- Security triage (Normal → Critical), prompt-injection resistance
- Event/person dedup across cameras, multi-turn context carry-over
- JSON compliance, PII redaction, alert routing
- VLM-to-alert end-to-end triage (fixture images are AI-generated)

Why it matters
- Strong evidence that a well-tuned 9B local model can deliver near–frontier accuracy on a concrete vertical (home security), with privacy and predictable cost.
- Practical latencies: some local MoE models show faster time-to-first-token than cloud, though cloud still dominates throughput.

Caveats
- It’s a creator-made, domain-specific benchmark; results may not generalize to open-ended chat.
- Vision fixtures are synthetic; real-world noise and edge cases may differ.
- One cloud baseline (GPT-5-mini) underperformed due to API temperature constraints, noted by the authors.

Code/benchmark: available on GitHub; live run video included.
