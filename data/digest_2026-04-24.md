**Theory Lagging Behind Practice**
While the submission was praised for providing a solid summary of research directions and open problems, several commenters pointed out a recurring frustration: theory in deep learning usually arrives retroactively to explain why empirical tweaks worked, rather than proactively guiding optimal network design. Furthermore, some users expressed exasperation that the rapidly moving AI field often struggles to "re-learn" foundational principles from other disciplines, noting that many current modeling challenges could be addressed by decades-old perspectives in non-equilibrium thermodynamics and mathematics.

**The Mystery of Over-parameterization**
A major part of the discussion centered on *why* neural networks actually work, despite violating classical statistical laws. Commenters highlighted the paradox of "double descent" and over-parameterization. According to classical statistical learning (like the bias-variance tradeoff famously detailed by Hastie et al.), these massive neural networks should overfit and fail. That they don't is an incredibly useful empirical success, but one that continues to "bug" theorists seeking a rigorous explanation. 

**Universal Approximation vs. The "Secret Sauce" (Compression & Grokking)**
Some users questioned whether the Universal Approximation Theorem explains deep learning's success. However, others quickly pushed back, pointing out that infinite-dimensional Hilbert space models (like SVMs, RBFs, and gradient-boosted decision trees) also possess this property. Therefore, universal approximation alone isn't what makes deep learning special.

Instead, the community argued that the "secret sauce" is **implicit regularization and compression**. The discussion heavily referenced the "grokking" phenomenon—where a model suddenly learns to generalize long after memorizing the training data. There was a lively debate over the mechanics behind this: while some argued grokking requires explicit L1 regularization to push weights toward zero (viewed as compressing via encoding data entirely in the fewest bits), others countered that modular addition networks can achieve grokking strictly through stable architecture design and implicit compression, sparking phase transitions in training.

**Escaping the Local Minima Trap**
Another core debate focused on gradient descent and the loss landscape. How do models avoid getting stuck in local minima during their "random walk" downhill? 
* **The High-Dimensional Geometry Argument:** A commenter pointed out that in spaces with hundreds of millions or billions of parameters, the mathematical probability of getting stuck in a true local minimum is remarkably small. For a point to be a local minimum, *all* gradient components must perfectly hit zero. Instead, Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) walks along vast manifolds.
* **Architecture Matters:** Users noted that optimization isn't just about the algorithm. Good initialization schemes, low-sharpness regions, and intelligent architecture design create a "funnel" or bowl-like loss landscape, providing a general trajectory that guides the optimizer toward good solutions.

**Defending Interpretability**
Finally, there was pushback against the defeatist attitude that neural networks are inherently "hopeless black boxes." Pointing to classically interpretable models like linear regression, a few users argued that the engineering side of AI is simply moving faster than the research side. They urged patience, noting that mechanistic interpretability is hard work and researchers shouldn't give up simply because the field doesn't yield immediately gratifying answers.

### Different language models learn similar number representations

#### [Submission URL](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.20817) | 111 points | by [Anon84](https://news.ycombinator.com/user?id=Anon84) | [46 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47890873)

Convergent evolution in number sense: Across very different language models—Transformers, LSTMs, linear RNNs, and even classic word embeddings—numbers end up encoded with strikingly similar periodic structure. Analyzing embeddings in the Fourier domain, the authors find dominant periodic features at T = 2, 5, and 10, mirroring common numeric bases (and the factors of 10).

Key findings
- Two-tier structure: Many models show clear period-T spikes in the Fourier spectrum of number representations, but only some arrange numbers so that residues mod T are linearly separable in embedding space. They prove Fourier sparsity is necessary but not sufficient for this geometric separability.
- What makes separability emerge: Data, architecture, optimizer, and tokenizer all matter. Models can acquire separable mod-T features via:
  1) Natural text signals (e.g., text-number co-occurrence and interactions between numbers in context).
  2) Training on multi-token addition tasks; interestingly, single-token addition does not produce the same effect.
- Convergent evolution: Despite different training signals and architectures, models learn notably similar number features.

Why it matters
- Interpretability: Offers a concrete, testable account of how LMs internalize numeric structure and why they often handle parity/last-digit patterns well.
- Training insights: If you want robust, linearly decodable numeric features, include cross-number interactions or multi-token arithmetic; tokenizer choices for numbers can be pivotal.
- Limits of “spectral” signals: Simply seeing Fourier periodicity isn’t enough—you need the right geometry for easy downstream use.

Paper: Convergent Evolution: How Different Language Models Learn Similar Number Representations (Fu, Zhou, Belkin, Sharan, Jia). arXiv:2604.20817, Apr 22, 2026. DOI: https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2604.20817

*   **Contextual memory:** The AI remembers your preferences, tasks, and work context across different sessions.
*   **Rich tooling:** Built-in access to web scraping (Firecrawl), image generation (Replicate), headless browsing, and shell execution.
*   **Model-agnostic & Git-backed:** You can swap between top-tier OpenAI and Anthropic models mid-conversation, and optionally sync/version-control your entire workspace using Git. 

**The Discussion:**
Despite the impressive feature set, the Hacker News discussion fixated entirely on the project's name. Commenters were quick to point out a massive historical naming collision: **LILO** is already famously known in the tech world as the legacy Linux bootloader (Linux Loader), which was introduced back in 1992. Users heavily criticized the name choice as confusing for an OS-related project, jokingly citing a failure to do a basic web search and reminding everyone of the classic computer science adage that "naming things is hard."
