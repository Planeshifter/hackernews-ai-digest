### Different language models learn similar number representations

#### [Submission URL](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.20817) | 94 points | by [Anon84](https://news.ycombinator.com/user?id=Anon84) | [38 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47890873)

Convergent Evolution: Different LMs independently learn the same number features (arXiv:2604.20817)

TL;DR: Across architectures and training setups, language models tend to encode numbers with periodic features aligned to bases 2, 5, and 10. But only some models turn those signals into clean, linearly separable representations for classifying numbers mod T—revealing a gap between having “spiky” Fourier features and having usable geometric structure.

What they found
- Universal periodicity: Transformers, Linear RNNs, LSTMs, and even classic word embeddings learn number features with strong Fourier spikes at periods T = 2, 5, 10.
- Two-tier structure: Fourier sparsity is necessary but not sufficient for clean mod-T separability. You can see the periodic spikes without getting linearly separable number classes.
- Training matters: Data, architecture, optimizer, and tokenizer all influence whether models develop geometrically separable number features.
- Two routes to separability:
  1) Natural text signals (e.g., text–number co-occurrence and cross-number interactions).
  2) Synthetic arithmetic—but it requires multi-token addition; single-token addition doesn’t induce the same structure.

Why it matters
- Numeracy in LMs isn’t just about periodic signals; turning those signals into linearly useful structure depends on choices in data and training.
- Practical levers: Tokenizer design, curriculum (multi-token arithmetic), and optimizer/architecture choices can improve downstream arithmetic and modular reasoning.
- Theory takeaway: The paper formalizes that Fourier sparsity is necessary but not enough for mod-T geometric separability, clarifying why “pretty spectra” don’t always translate to usable numeric skills.

Big picture: A diverse set of models converges on similar number encodings from different signals—convergent evolution—but only some pipelines shape those encodings into features that are linearly usable.
