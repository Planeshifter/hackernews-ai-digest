### Parallel coding agents with tmux and Markdown specs

#### [Submission URL](https://schipper.ai/posts/parallel-coding-agents/) | 167 points | by [schipperai](https://news.ycombinator.com/user?id=schipperai) | [129 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47218318)

A lightweight, spec-first workflow for parallel coding agents without heavy orchestration. One engineer has been running 4–8 “vanilla” agents using tmux windows (Planner, Worker, PM), Markdown, and six bash slash commands—no subagents or external orchestrators—anchored by Feature Designs (FDs), numbered specs that drive all work and traceability.

What’s in the system
- Feature Designs (FDs): Each md file captures the problem, alternative solutions with pros/cons, the chosen approach with a file-level implementation plan, and verification steps. Commits are tagged to their FD, and the changelog builds automatically as FDs complete.
- Lifecycle and tracking: FDs live in docs/features/ as FD-001, FD-002… and move through eight stages (Planned → Design → Open → In Progress → Pending Verification → Complete → Deferred → Closed), indexed in FEATURE_INDEX.md.
- Six slash commands:
  - /fd-new: Create an FD from an idea dump
  - /fd-status: Show active, pending verification, and done
  - /fd-explore: Load architecture docs, dev guide, and FD index to bootstrap context
  - /fd-deep: Launch 4 parallel Opus agents to explore a hard design
  - /fd-verify: Proofread, propose verification, commit
  - /fd-close: Archive FD, update index and changelog
- /fd-init bootstrap: Ports the system to any repo by inferring context (CLAUDE.md, package configs, git log), creating docs/features/, archive/, FEATURE_INDEX.md, an FD template, CLAUDE.md conventions, CHANGELOG.md, and .claude/commands/*.

Why it works
- Most coding happens only after a finished spec, enabling safe parallelization and clearer handoffs between Planner and Worker roles.
- The author ran 300+ specs this way; 4–8 agents is the sweet spot—beyond that, decision quality drops.
- Everything stays local and auditable in plain text: easy to adopt, easy to port, and high traceability from idea to changelog.

Your Language Model Secretly Contains Personality Subnetworks (arXiv, ICLR 2026)

TL;DR: The authors claim LLMs already contain “persona” circuits in their parameters. With a small calibration set, they identify activation signatures for a given persona and apply a masking/pruning scheme to isolate a lightweight subnetwork that reliably steers the model’s behavior—no fine-tuning, RAG, or extra context tokens required.

What’s new
- Persona subnetworks: Evidence that different “personas” (e.g., helpful, sarcastic, formal) correspond to distinct activation patterns already embedded in the model.
- Training-free control: A simple, statistics-guided masking approach isolates these persona-specific subnetworks using only a small calibration dataset.
- Binary opposites: For pairs like introvert–extrovert, a contrastive pruning method finds parameters driving the divergence between opposing behaviors, further separating the two modes.

How it works
- Collect activation stats for a target persona from a small set of examples.
- Use these stats to build masks that keep parameters most implicated in that persona’s behavior, effectively “routing” the model through a persona subnetwork at inference.
- For opposing personas, apply contrastive pruning to retain parameters that differentiate the two, sharpening the behavioral split.

Results (per the paper)
- Stronger persona alignment than baselines that rely on prompts, RAG, or fine-tuning.
- More efficient at inference because it avoids long persona prompts and any additional training.
- Works across “diverse evaluation settings” (details not in the abstract).

Why it matters
- Practical personalization without prompt bloat, adapters, or extra compute.
- A step toward interpretable control: personas as tangible, toggleable subnetworks rather than opaque prompt engineering.
- Potentially useful for safety modes, brand voice, or multi-persona agents on resource-constrained devices.

Caveats and open questions
- How robust is the alignment across domains, languages, and models? Stability under adversarial prompting?
- What’s the size of the “small” calibration set, and how sensitive are results to its composition?
- Safety implications of making it easier to elicit specific behaviors, including undesirable ones.
- Reproducibility and breadth of model coverage aren’t clear from the abstract.
