### Ultrafast machine learning on FPGAs via Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks

#### [Submission URL](https://aarushgupta.io/posts/kan-fpga/) | 275 points | by [ag2718](https://news.ycombinator.com/user?id=ag2718) | [46 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48466277)

Ultrafast ML on FPGAs via Kolmogorov–Arnold Networks (KANs). A master’s thesis (with two companion papers, including an FPGA 2026 Best Paper) shows how KANs map unusually well to FPGA hardware, enabling nanosecond–sub‑microsecond inference and even on‑device online learning.

Why FPGAs, not GPUs
- Deterministic, ultra‑low latency and high efficiency for specialized workloads, without the scheduling/memory overheads of processors.
- Neural nets are realized as circuits, not instructions—ideal when you can co‑design algorithm and hardware.

Core idea
- Represent learned functions as lookup tables (LUTs) under fixed‑point quantization. FPGAs natively excel at LUT evaluation and pipelining.
- Multivariate LUTs explode combinatorially, but KANs sidestep this via the Kolmogorov–Arnold representation: complex multivariate functions are composed from sums of 1D functions. Those 1D functions (splines) are perfect for compact LUTs.

What’s new
- KANELÉ (FPGA 2026 Best Paper): an architecture for efficient LUT‑based evaluation of KANs on FPGAs—turning spline activations into fast, deeply pipelined LUT logic with fixed‑point arithmetic.
- ICML 2026 paper: online learning on FPGA by exploiting spline locality—only a few LUT entries update per sample, enabling ultrafast in‑place adaptation.

Why it matters
- Deterministic, hardware‑friendly ML with tiny latency and strong energy efficiency.
- Supports both inference and real‑time updates, opening doors for control, HFT, scientific triggers, and other “react in nanoseconds” use cases.

Takeaway
By quantizing KANs and compiling their 1D spline pieces into LUTs, the work delivers a practical recipe for ultrafast inference and on‑FPGA online learning—leveraging FPGAs’ strengths while avoiding the usual LUT scaling pitfalls.

What’s new: A small but pointed empirical study asks a deceptively simple question: in LLM agent loops, does plain grep outperform vector search? On a 116-question slice of LongMemEval, the authors compare retrieval strategies across different agent “harnesses” (the scaffolding that runs tools and feeds outputs back to the model) and tool-calling styles.

How they tested:
- Setups: a custom agent harness (Chronos) and provider-native CLIs (Claude Code, Codex, Gemini CLI).
- Retrieval: grep vs vector search.
- Tool I/O styles: inline results vs file-based results the model reads separately.
- Stress test: progressively inject unrelated conversation history to see how retrieval holds up when relevant text is buried in distractors.

Key findings (from the abstract):
- Grep generally yields higher accuracy than vector retrieval in Experiment 1 across Chronos and the provider CLIs on the same underlying conversation data.
- Overall scores vary strongly by harness and how tool outputs are presented (inline vs file), suggesting the agent wrapper and I/O design can outweigh the choice of retriever.
- Experiment 2 benchmarks grep-only vs vector-only while increasing irrelevant context; the abstract emphasizes sensitivity to distractors and harness design but does not report detailed results there.

Why it matters:
- Don’t assume vectors win by default—especially in agent loops over known corpora. A strong keyword baseline can be both simpler and more effective.
- The agent harness (prompting, tool orchestration, and how outputs are fed back) is a first-class performance lever; benchmarking retrieval in isolation can be misleading.
- Reporting harness details should be standard in RAG/agent papers and product evals.

Caveats:
- Small sample (116 questions) and specific harnesses/tools; results may not generalize to other domains, corpora, or embedding configs.
- The abstract highlights trends but withholds many numbers; full paper needed for effect sizes and methodology specifics.
