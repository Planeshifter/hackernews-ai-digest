Abhishek Ray argues that as AI agents (e.g., Claude Code + Gastown) churn out far more PRs than humans can review, “self-congratulation” testing—having the same model write code and tests—doesn’t validate intent. His fix: revive TDD’s spirit with acceptance-criteria-first verification that’s independent of the coding agent.

How it works
- Write acceptance criteria in plain English before prompting the agent (e.g., login redirects, exact error strings, rate limits).
- Let the agent implement the feature.
- Verify behavior with Playwright (frontend) or curl checks (backend), capturing screenshots/evidence.
- Review only failures instead of diffs.

The tool
- verify (github.com/opslane/verify) is a Claude Skill running via claude -p (headless) + Playwright MCP; no extra backend or keys beyond Claude OAuth.
- Pipeline:
  1) Pre-flight (bash): sanity checks without LLM tokens.
  2) Planner (one Opus call): reads spec and code, maps checks, finds selectors.
  3) Browser agents (one Sonnet call per AC, in parallel) to execute and record evidence.
  4) Judge (final Opus call): returns pass/fail/needs-review per criterion with reasoning.
- Installable as a Claude Code plugin (opslane/verify) and CI-friendly.

Why it matters
- Scales trust when you can’t review every diff.
- Catches integration/rendering issues real browsers see, not just unit-test theory.
- Honest limitation: if the spec is wrong, verification will still “pass”—this reduces review scope, not product ambiguity.

Tagline: Write ACs first, let agents build, auto-verify, and only read the failures.

### Levels of Agentic Engineering

#### [Submission URL](https://www.bassimeledath.com/blog/levels-of-agentic-engineering) | 252 points | by [bombastic311](https://news.ycombinator.com/user?id=bombastic311) | [126 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47320614)

Title: The 8 Levels of Agentic Engineering: closing the gap between AI capability and real productivity

Why it matters:
- Benchmarks like SWE-bench aren’t mapping to the metrics leaders care about. Teams using the same models can have wildly different outcomes (e.g., Anthropic shipping Cowork in 10 days vs. others stuck at a broken POC).
- Productivity is “multiplayer”: your throughput depends on your teammates’ level. A level-7 engineer bottlenecked by a level-2 reviewer loses most of the gains.

The progression (excerpt focuses on Levels 1–4):
- Levels 1–2: Tab-complete (Copilot) and Agent IDEs (Cursor). Big step forward via chat + codebase context and plan mode, but still capped by context limits and noisy prompts.
- Level 3: Context engineering. Make every token earn its place. Key levers:
  - Tight system prompts/rules (CLAUDE.md, .cursorrules)
  - Precise tool descriptions and selective tool exposure per turn
  - Conversation-history management so agents don’t lose the plot
  - Still critical when using smaller models, voice, or token-hungry tools (Playwright, images) and when many tools bloat schema parsing
  - Shift from “filter bad context” to “ensure the right context at the right time”
- Level 4: Compounding engineering. A loop: plan → delegate → assess → codify.
  - The codify step turns wins/mistakes into reusable guidance so sessions get better over time
  - Don’t overstuff rules files; maintain discoverable, up-to-date docs/ that the model can pull in
  - Practitioners default to “what context was missing?” before blaming the model

Takeaways for teams:
- Treat AI productivity as a maturity curve; invest in pulling the whole team up levels.
- Prune and scope tools; gate which ones are visible per turn.
- Keep rules concise; push patterns and decisions into easily retrievable docs.
- Build the compounding loop into your workflow so today’s lessons become tomorrow’s defaults.

The discussion examines the gap between theoretical "agentic" levels and current practical realities, focusing on context management, the economics of automation, and specific technical hurdles.

**The "Context Rot" Bottleneck & File-Based Memory**
The most significant technical hurdle discussed is "context rot" for agents operating at Level 5+. Users note that after ~20 minutes or long sessions, agents rediscover architecture or hallucinate previously corrected assumptions due to context window limits.
*   **The Consensus Solution:** Convergence toward **file-based state persistence** (e.g., updating a `STATE.md` or `specs.md` file) rather than relying on chat context.
*   **Mechanism:** Agents record decisions, checked items, and plans to disk. Subsequent agent invocations read this file to "remember" status. This is cheaper (fewer tokens) and treats the plan as the source of truth.
*   **Review Benefit:** This enables "Plan vs. Implementation" reviews, where humans review the intent/spec rather than just guessing the intent behind generated code.

**Skepticism of the "Software Factory" (Levels 5-8)**
Commenters debated the economic implications of fully automated "dark factories."
*   **The "Sell the Factory" Argument:** Skeptics argue that if a company achieves a Level 8 "software factory" capable of autonomous development, they should sell the factory itself (higher TAM) rather than the software it produces.
*   **Counterpoint:** Coding is only a fraction of a business. Even with automated engineering, companies still compete on sales, marketing, infrastructure, physical goods, and legal positioning.
*   **Differentiation:** If code becomes a commodity, value shifts entirely to brand, customer relationships, and proprietary data.

**Frontend vs. Backend & Environment Complexity**
Users differentiated where these agents succeed:
*   **Scaffolding & Isolation:** Agents excel in "dumb environments" (isolated classes, single components) or greenfield projects.
*   **Frontend Limitations:** UX and Frontend work are described as a "hot mess" for agents; describing complex UI interactions via text prompts is often harder than writing the code.
*   **Legacy Code:** Complex, existing environments remain a barrier compared to "copy-paste" tasks on fresh stacks.

**Novice vs. Expert Workflow**
*   **Skill Atrophy:** Novices expressed concern that relying on agents stunts personal growth and understanding of the codebase.
*   **Security Risks:** While agents can replace manual review for syntax, they introduce "subtle mistakes" (logic/security flows) that require high-level human oversight. Deep security reviews become *more* critical, not less.

  - Full bidirectional models (VGGT, π3) handle local reasoning but don’t scale due to quadratic cost.

### Learnings from paying artists royalties for AI-generated art

#### [Submission URL](https://www.kapwing.com/blog/learnings-from-paying-artists-royalties-for-ai-generated-art/) | 166 points | by [jenthoven](https://news.ycombinator.com/user?id=jenthoven) | [160 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47318421)

Tess.Design tried to be the “properly licensed” Midjourney: a marketplace of fine‑tuned Stable Diffusion models where each image was traceable to a single consenting artist, who earned a 50% royalty per use. Backed by Fenwick-drafted agreements, Tess argued that outputs were stylistically transformed derivatives owned by the contributing artist, creating a clean chain of copyright for risk-averse publishers.

Go-to-market and artist supply:
- Seeded with 25 founding artists via upfront advances of $300–$4,000, plus a 50% revenue share, usage dashboards, and a free subscription.
- 325 cold emails to high-end editorial illustrators yielded a ~50% response rate; 6.5% joined, 22.4% were hard nos, 20.8% stayed on the fence, 50.2% didn’t reply.
- By 2025, Tess had ~37 contributing artists. About 1 in 4 used Tess themselves to speed repetitive work.

Why many artists refused:
- Ideological opposition to generative AI (“no such thing as ethical AI”).
- Brand dilution concerns and loss of control over where their style appears.
- Principle: art should be made by hand.
- Reputation risk and fear of being “canceled” for partnering with AI.

Traction and economics:
- Ran 20 months, generating $12,172.33 in gross revenue.
- Paid $18,000 in artist advances; infra cost about $100/month (initially offset by credits).
- Net result: roughly a $7,000 direct-cost loss (not counting founder time).

Takeaway: Even with a consent-first, royalty-paying model and enterprise-friendly legal framing, converting top-tier artists proved hard amid industry hostility and reputational risk, and demand/revenue didn’t cover advances—underscoring how difficult it is to build an AI licensing business on creative talent today.
