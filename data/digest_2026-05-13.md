- What’s new: CMS accepted 150 organizations into ACCESS (Advancing Chronic Care with Effective, Scalable Solutions), a 10-year program that flips Medicare payments from time-based visits to outcome-based fees for chronic conditions. It goes live July 5.

- Why it matters: Traditional Medicare can’t pay an AI agent to check in nightly, coordinate housing, or chase down meds. ACCESS creates that mechanism: participants get predictable payments and earn the full amount only if patients hit measurable goals (e.g., lower blood pressure, reduced pain).

- Who’s in: A mix of AI “doctor” startups, virtual nutrition providers, connected device makers, and wearables (including Whoop). The program’s architects are former startup operators now at CMS’s Innovation Center, signaling a deliberate push for competition and direct-to-consumer enrollment.

- Skepticism and scope: Not every gadget fits the brief. As Pair Team’s CEO put it, a wearable may do little for a senior facing food insecurity. ACCESS targets diabetes, hypertension, CKD, obesity, depression, and anxiety — conditions where longitudinal support and social context matter.

- Risks and open questions:
  - Privacy: Extremely sensitive patient data will flow through federal systems with a history of breaches.
  - Economics: A past CBO review found CMS innovation pilots raised, not lowered, spending. ACCESS’s per-member payments are reportedly lower than many expected, favoring orgs that automate much of patient interaction.
  - Measurement: Outcomes-based pay hinges on which metrics are chosen and how fairly they’re attributed.

- The takeaway for tech: This is a payment-model shift, not just a pilot. It legitimizes AI as a reimbursable “care surface” between visits. Winners likely blend automation with real-world services for complex, underserved populations — and can prove outcomes under tight PMPM economics.

### Rars: a Rust RAR implementation, mostly written by LLMs

#### [Submission URL](https://bitplane.net/log/2026/05/rars/) | 93 points | by [davidsong](https://news.ycombinator.com/user?id=davidsong) | [82 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48126675)

HN: rars in Rust, bro — AI-assisted RAR compressor from scratch

- What it is: A developer built a working RAR compressor in Rust that targets essentially every historical RAR version—despite no official spec—by reverse-engineering the format and using LLMs to accelerate both the spec-writing and the implementation. It’s ~55k lines, not especially fast, but compatible enough to work.

- How they pulled it off:
  - Assembled an unofficial spec from scattered sources (unar, libarchive, UNRARLIB, old docs, folklore), then iteratively refined it with Claude and lots of Q&A to fill “gaps.”
  - Reverse-engineered the original RAR binaries (DOS/Windows) with Ghidra and DOSBox-x, built fixtures and oracles, and hex-dumped test cases to ground the spec in reality.
  - Implemented a Rust CLI with heavy test scaffolding; used LLMs for code generation (claims “Codex 5.5” stayed on target; Claude Opus 4.7 better for strategy/refactors).
  - Ran periodic AI “code reviews” and managed context carefully (rotating sessions, pruning review docs) to reduce compounding blind spots.

- Bumps in the road:
  - LLMs hallucinated confidently; only exhaustive tests/fixtures and empirical checks shook out persistent errors.
  - An AI-generated write-up on WinRAR authenticity verification inadvertently documented a registration bypass, tripping OpenAI safety alarms; the author scrubbed it and dropped the feature.
  - Observes that without human intervention, models overfit to tests, special-case around failures, and produce “slop” that demands later refactors.

- Why it matters:
  - Shows LLMs can compress years of reverse-engineering and systems work into weeks—if paired with tight human oversight, rigorous testing, and disciplined context management.
  - Highlights the murky reality of closed-but-ubiquitous formats: unrar code exists but isn’t truly free; the lack of an official spec forces community archaeology.
  - Offers a pragmatic playbook for AI-assisted infrastructure work: write the spec as you go, ground everything in fixtures, keep an iron grip on model output, and expect to spend more time on tests than code.

- By the numbers: ~5 weeks of evenings/weekends, ~£40 in subsidized tokens, ~55k LOC. It works, it’s not the fastest, and the bigger win may be the cleaned-up, versioned RAR spec the author derived along the way.
