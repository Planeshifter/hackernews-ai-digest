Title: Fields medalist Timothy Gowers: ChatGPT 5.5 Pro produced a PhD-level result in additive number theory in ~1 hour

- Timothy Gowers reports that ChatGPT 5.5 Pro solved and wrote up a genuine research-level result in additive combinatorics in roughly an hour, with no substantive hints from him.
- Context: LLMs have been closing “easy but overlooked” open problems by recombining known techniques. Gowers argues the bar for “entry-level” open problems just went up: they now need to be hard enough that an LLM can’t dispatch them.
- The task: Given a set A of integers with prescribed |A| and |A+A| (or more generally mA), how large a diameter (range) is needed to realize those sizes? Mel Nathanson had shown existence with a certain bound and asked if it could be improved.
- ChatGPT’s contribution: After ~17 minutes, it gave a construction achieving a quadratic upper bound on the required diameter—optimal up to constants. It then produced a LaTeX preprint-style writeup in ~2 minutes.
- Key idea: Build A from a Sidon set (B2 set: all pairwise sums distinct, maximizing sumset size) plus an arithmetic progression, with a small tweak point for fine-tuning. Nathanson’s proof implicitly used a Sidon set of powers of 2; ChatGPT swapped in denser Sidon sets with quadratic diameter, yielding the optimal bound.
- Gowers checked the argument and found it correct. He frames this as the model not uncovering a deep new paradigm but executing solid, publishable mathematics by more efficiently combining standard tools.
- Takeaway: LLMs are now credible co-authors for certain combinatorial/additive problems, rapidly closing low-to-medium-hanging fruit. Human oversight and verification remain crucial, but “simple” open questions are increasingly within automated reach.

### LLMs corrupt your documents when you delegate

#### [Submission URL](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.15597) | 454 points | by [rbanffy](https://news.ycombinator.com/user?id=rbanffy) | [177 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48073246)

LLMs Corrupt Your Documents When You Delegate (arXiv:2604.15597)
- What’s new: Researchers introduce DELEGATE-52, a benchmark that simulates long delegated editing workflows across 52 professional domains (e.g., coding, crystallography, music notation) to test whether LLMs can be trusted as autonomous document editors.
- Key result: In tests with 19 LLMs, even frontier models (Gemini 3.1 Pro, Claude 4.6 Opus, GPT 5.4 per the paper) degraded documents, corrupting about 25% of content on average by the end of long workflows; many models did worse.
- Why it happens: Errors are sparse but severe, often silent, and compound over many steps. Degradation worsens with larger documents, longer interactions, and the presence of distractor files.
- Tools didn’t save them: Agentic/tool-using setups did not improve performance on DELEGATE-52.
- Takeaway: Today’s LLMs are unreliable as hands-off delegates for complex, multi-step editing. If you must delegate, enforce tight scopes and verification: diff-based reviews, tests/linters, file locking, small atomic changes, and frequent checkpoints.

**Today's Top Story: Do LLMs Corrupt Your Documents?**
A new paper, *LLMs Corrupt Your Documents When You Delegate* (arXiv:2604.15597), introduced a benchmark called DELEGATE-52 to test how well LLMs handle autonomous, long-form document editing across 52 professional domains. The researchers found that across 19 models—including frontier models like GPT, Claude, and Gemini—relying on LLMs for hands-off, multi-step editing resulted in a 25% degradation of content. *   **The Human Baseline:** One user pointed out the absurdity of the test by comparing it to humans: if you asked a human to read a document and then rewrite the entire thing from scratch without using "find-and-replace" tools, the human would also suffer a massive content degradation rate.

While developers scoffed at the paper’s lack of modern agentic tooling, others defended the findings by pointing out user reality. 
*   **The Copilot Reality:** The vast majority of people do not use sophisticated coding harnesses. Others countered that the software industry has spent the last 15 years selling AI as an omnipotent "magic" assistant that requires zero training, setting false expectations for non-technical users. 

**3. Tooling Debates & AI Bloat**
