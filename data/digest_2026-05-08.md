### AI is breaking two vulnerability cultures

#### [Submission URL](https://www.jefftk.com/p/ai-is-breaking-two-vulnerability-cultures) | 386 points | by [speckx](https://news.ycombinator.com/user?id=speckx) | [154 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48066524)

AI is Breaking Two Vulnerability Cultures (Jeff Kaufman)

- The spark: After the “Copy Fail” Linux networking vuln, Hyunwoo Kim quietly landed a fix following Linux’s norm—notify a closed security list, commit a minimal patch publicly, and rely on a short informal embargo. Someone spotted the change, flagged the security angle, and the embargo effectively ended. Separately, Kuan-Ting Chen independently reported the related ESP issue just nine hours after Kim—evidence that parallel discovery is fast now.

- The clash: 
  - Coordinated disclosure: private report, generous window (often ~90 days) to ship a fix before details go public.
  - “Bugs are bugs” (common in Linux): fix quickly in the open without fanfare, hoping the flood of commits keeps attackers from noticing.

- How AI upends both:
  - Commit-watching gets teeth: LLMs can already spot likely security patches from commits/diffs, making low-key fixes easier to detect.
  - Long embargoes are riskier: multiple AI-assisted teams are scanning the same code, so independent rediscovery within hours/days is likelier, and embargoes can create false non-urgency while limiting who can help.

- The author’s take:
  - Shift to much shorter embargoes, shrinking over time.
  - Lean on AI to speed triage, patching, and rollout so short embargoes are still useful.
  - Assume every public commit may be scrutinized by machines; silent fixes won’t stay silent.

- Mini experiment: Given a specific commit, Gemini, GPT, and Claude quickly inferred it was a security fix; even with only a diff, two models still leaned “security-relevant.” Not rigorous, but a sign of where things are headed.

Bottom line: The discovery-to-public window is collapsing. Defenders should optimize for rapid, AI-augmented fixes with minimal embargo, and stop counting on obscurity-in-commits.

- How they now align: Combine constitutional documents, high-quality chat that demonstrates constitutional responses to hard dilemmas, and diverse environments (“honeypot” scenarios) to cut misalignment on held-out automated assessments.

- Why it matters: It’s a concrete recipe—teach principles + reasons, not just outcomes—that appears to transfer better. If it holds up under adversarial red-teaming and real deployments, it’s a notable step toward safer agentic AI.

### Can LLMs model real-world systems in TLA+?

#### [Submission URL](https://www.sigops.org/2026/can-llms-model-real-world-systems-in-tla/) | 111 points | by [mad](https://news.ycombinator.com/user?id=mad) | [28 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48065254)

Specula introduces SysMoBench, a benchmark to test whether LLMs can model real systems in TLA+—not just regurgitate textbook specs. The team first asked Claude to write a TLA+ spec for etcd’s Raft; it compiled, ran under TLC, and looked polished—until they realized it was effectively the Raft paper’s appendix, not etcd’s actual implementation.

SysMoBench supplies 11 real systems (from synchronization to distributed protocols) with code, a trace harness, and invariant templates, then evaluates LLM-generated specs in four phases: syntax, runtime, trace-based conformance to implementations, and safety/liveness invariants. Today’s top models ace syntax/runtime but stumble on faithfulness: average ~46% on conformance and ~41% on invariants.

Two recurring failure modes explain the gap:
- Admitting impossible states by using “textbook” data structures or updates. Example: modeling ZooKeeper FLE’s received votes as an ever-growing set rather than a per-sender map that overwrites older votes, creating states the real system never enters.
- Blocking reachable states by collapsing multi-step code paths into a single atomic guard. Example: treating “only process if incoming epoch ≤ local” as one check, instead of the implementation’s two-step “bump local epoch, then process,” which erases routinely visited states.

Takeaway: passing syntax and TLC execution is insufficient. Faithful modeling requires conformance against real execution traces and attention to how implementations decompose actions and evolve state. SysMoBench’s action- and invariant-level diagnostics aim to measure modeling—not memorization—and set a bar for agentic model checking and applied formal methods with LLMs.

### Human typing habits and token counts

#### [Submission URL](https://pankajpipada.com/posts/2026-05-08-human-habits-tokens/) | 27 points | by [ppipada](https://news.ycombinator.com/user?id=ppipada) | [4 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48062606)

Human Typing Habits and Token Counts

TL;DR: Your everyday typing quirks can quietly raise LLM bills. Common spellings compress into fewer tokens; typos, shorthands, filler, and pasted IDs fragment into more—often without adding meaning. The author finds Claude’s tokenizer tends to emit more tokens than OpenAI’s for the same text.

Highlights
- Typos are billable: template → 1 token vs tempalte → 3; loaded → 1 vs lodaed → 2 (Claude: 3). Rare forms fragment; common forms compress. In code, a bad identifier repeats everywhere and compounds cost.
- Word shapes matter: tiny suffixes change splits (describe → 1; describer → 2; describers → 3; error → 1; errored → 2).
- Conversation padding adds up: fillers/hedges/wrappers and expressive tails like Good... (2 vs 1), Yes!! (2 vs 1), yesss (3) increase tokens with little task value.
- Shorthands aren’t cheaper: please (1) vs pls (2), thanks (1) vs thx (2), without (1) vs w/o (2; Claude: 3). Standard words are usually both clearer and cheaper.
- Quiet token leaks: UUIDs (e.g., 24–26 tokens), RFC 3339 timestamps (16–17), long URLs/paths, plus leading/trailing whitespace. Counts here are for isolated strings; surrounding spaces, punctuation, and casing can shift totals.
- Tokenizer differences: In the author’s tests, Claude generally splits the same text into more tokens than OpenAI.

Why it matters
- Meaning may be unchanged, but billing isn’t. For prompts, code, and chats, small habits become line items.

Practical takeaways
- Favor correct, standard spelling and drop low-signal padding.
- Use full words over shorthands.
- Trim boundary whitespace.
- Be mindful of pasted IDs, timestamps, and long URLs—summarize or reference when possible.
- Expect provider/tokenizer variance when estimating costs.

### Show HN: Git for AI Agents

#### [Submission URL](https://github.com/regent-vcs/re_gent) | 113 points | by [doshay](https://news.ycombinator.com/user?id=doshay) | [62 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48063548)

re_gent: “git for AI agents” that audits every tool call and ties code changes back to the exact prompt. The open-source CLI (rgt/regent) auto-captures agent edits, shell commands, and test writes—no manual commits—so you can see what changed, why, and by which session.

Why it matters
- AI agents are editing real code, but there’s no provenance. re_gent adds auditability, accountability, and an easy way to rewind when things go sideways.

How it works
- Tracks each tool call as a Step with parent, workspace snapshot, tool name/args/result, timestamp, session ID, and a conversation delta.
- Stores data in .regent/ using content-addressed blobs (BLAKE3) plus a SQLite index; sessions form a DAG per agent (like branches) with deduped ancestors.
- Core primitives:
  - rgt log — see the step-by-step history
  - rgt blame file:line — which prompt wrote this line
  - rgt sessions — track concurrent agent timelines
  - rgt show <step> — full context for any change
  - rgt rewind — restore to any previous step (coming soon)

Developer experience
- “Every tool call is automatically captured.” No workflow changes; keep using your agent (e.g., Claude) and code normally.
- Demo outputs include per-step diffs (+/- lines), tool used (Edit/Write/Bash), and prompt text that caused the change.
- VSCode extension adds inline blame, hover tooltips with step metadata, and a session timeline.

Install
- Homebrew: brew tap regent-vcs/tap && brew install regent
- Go: go install github.com/regent-vcs/regent/cmd/rgt@latest
- Prebuilt binaries and VSIX for the VSCode extension are on GitHub (regent-vcs/regent and regent-vcs/vscode-regent).

State of play
- Works today for logging, blame, sessions, and rich step inspection; rewind and Marketplace listing are noted as “coming soon.”

Big picture
- re_gent complements git by adding agent-level provenance—mapping prompts to lines—so teams can audit, debug, and confidently let AI write code.

### Hallucinations Undermine Trust; Metacognition Is a Way Forward

#### [Submission URL](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.01428) | 18 points | by [gmays](https://news.ycombinator.com/user?id=gmays) | [6 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48064373)

Hallucinations Undermine Trust; Metacognition is a Way Forward (arXiv, ICML ’26 position paper) — Yona, Geva, Matias

- Core claim: Despite better factuality, frontier LLMs still hallucinate even on simple QA. Most gains come from stuffing in more facts (bigger knowledge boundary), not from knowing where that boundary is. Perfectly separating true from false may be inherently hard, so trying to “eliminate hallucinations” without harming usefulness hits a wall.

- Reframe: Treat hallucinations as confident errors. Instead of the answer-or-abstain binary, add a third option: express calibrated uncertainty.

- Proposal: “Faithful uncertainty” — align what the model says about confidence (hedges, probabilities, citations) with its intrinsic uncertainty signals. That’s one facet of metacognition: knowing what you don’t know and acting on it.

- For chat: honestly communicate uncertainty so users can judge trust. For agents: use metacognition as a control layer to decide when to search, verify, or defer — and what sources to trust.

- Why it matters: Shifts the goal from zero hallucinations to trustworthy behavior. Implies new evals and product metrics that reward calibration and honest signaling, not just accuracy or abstention rate.

- Likely debates on HN: 
  - Users’ tolerance for hedging vs desire for crisp answers
  - How to measure “faithful” uncertainty and prevent performative disclaimers
  - UI/UX for confidence without clutter
  - Training/incentive design so models aren’t overconfident (or timid)
  - Interplay with RAG/tools: when to trigger search and how to fuse confidence across sources

TL;DR: You can’t perfectly purge hallucinations without losing utility; make models metacognitive so they say “how sure” they are — and let that govern when they speak, search, or stay silent.
