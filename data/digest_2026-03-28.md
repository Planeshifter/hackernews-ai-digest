### Go hard on agents, not on your filesystem

#### [Submission URL](https://jai.scs.stanford.edu/) | 605 points | by [mazieres](https://news.ycombinator.com/user?id=mazieres) | [313 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47550282)

jai: a one-command sandbox to keep AI agents from nuking your files on Linux

Why it matters: As AI coding tools become more hands-on, users keep reporting “oops” moments—emptied working trees, wiped home directories, even whole drives gone—after running agents with normal machine access. jai, from Stanford’s Secure Computer Systems group, aims to shrink that blast radius without the friction of containers or VMs.

What it does:
- Drop-in containment for agents and scripts: run “jai your-agent” (or just “jai” for a shell).
- Your current working directory stays fully writable.
- Your home directory is mounted copy-on-write (originals untouched) or can be hidden entirely.
- Everything else is read-only; /tmp and /var/tmp are private.

Modes to fit risk and convenience:
- Casual: runs as your user; home is COW; easy, but weak confidentiality.
- Strict: runs as an unprivileged jai user with a private empty home; stronger isolation.
- Bare: runs as your user with an empty private home; medium confidentiality, home hidden.
- Notes: NFS homes supported in Casual and Bare; not in Strict.

How it compares:
- Docker: great for reproducible images, heavier for ad‑hoc host tool sandboxing; no overlay-on-home workflow.
- bubblewrap: powerful but fiddly (lots of flags); jai removes that setup friction.
- chroot: not a security boundary; lacks namespaces and isolation jai relies on.

Caveats:
- It’s a “casual sandbox,” not perfect security. Casual mode doesn’t protect confidentiality, and even Strict isn’t a substitute for hardened containers or VMs when facing determined adversaries.

Free and open-source, built to make “just run this command” safer for quick coding help, one-off tasks, and untrusted installer scripts—without stopping to build images or maintain Dockerfiles.

### Be careful: chatting with AI about your case is discoverable

#### [Submission URL](https://harvardlawreview.org/blog/2026/03/united-states-v-heppner/) | 31 points | by [rogerallen](https://news.ycombinator.com/user?id=rogerallen) | [10 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47555642)

Harvard Law Review: Judge says chats with Claude aren’t privileged (U.S. v. Heppner)

In a first-of-its-kind ruling, SDNY Judge Jed Rakoff held that a criminal defendant’s self-directed exchanges with Anthropic’s Claude aren’t protected by attorney-client privilege or the work-product doctrine. Agents seized ~31 “AI documents” where the defendant fed facts learned from his lawyers into Claude to draft defense ideas he later shared with counsel. The court said the chats fail privilege on multiple fronts: they weren’t communications with an attorney; there was no reasonable expectation of confidentiality given Anthropic’s data and disclosure policies; and the defendant couldn’t have been seeking legal advice from Claude (which itself disclaims giving legal advice). The work-product claim also failed because the materials weren’t prepared by or at counsel’s direction or reflective of counsel’s strategy.

The HLR piece argues the opinion goes too far toward a categorical bar. A more fact-specific approach could protect AI-assisted client work in some scenarios—especially if counsel directs the use and the AI functions as the lawyer’s agent under Kovel, and if true confidentiality is maintained.

Why it matters:
- Early precedent: Client use of consumer AI can blow privilege and work product.
- Confidentiality is king: Provider policies, training use, and third-party access weighed heavily.
- Process design: Privilege may be salvageable if lawyers direct AI use, use enterprise tools with strict privacy, and integrate AI as an agent within the attorney-client relationship.

Here is a summary of the Hacker News discussion regarding the *U.S. v. Heppner* ruling on AI and attorney-client privilege:

**The Core Debate: Is AI a "Tool" or a "Third Party"?**
The discussion highlighted a clear divide between how technologists view AI and how the law views it. Many HN commenters argued that Claude should simply be viewed as a modern drafting tool. They compared the defendant’s use of Claude to brainstorming in a browser-based word processor, saving drafts in a cloud webmail account, or using text-to-speech software. To these users, if the *intent* of the document is to prepare a defense for legal counsel, the technological medium shouldn't matter. 

**Legal Pragmatists Weigh In: It's a Narrow Ruling**
Commenters with a legal background pushed back against the outrage, noting that the ruling is actually quite narrow and logically sound based on existing law. They clarified a crucial distinction: in this case, the attorney was *not* involved in setting up or directing the use of the AI. Because the defendant independently "conversed" with a non-lawyer entity (Claude), it is legally no different than brainstorming legal strategy with a non-lawyer friend or family member. Privilege was never meant to cover independent third-party discussions just because they are eventually handed over to a lawyer.

**Cloud Privacy Policies Are Under the Microscope**
A major point of interest was Judge Rakoff’s reliance on Anthropic’s fine-print privacy policies to determine that the defendant had no "reasonable expectation" of confidentiality.
*   **The Model Training Trap:** Commenters noted the judge specifically highlighted Anthropic's practice of training its models on user data. Users agreed that if a platform actively ingests your inputs to train public models, you cannot claim you expected the data to remain secret. 
*   **Thorny Implications for Cloud Software:** Some users pointed out that this raises tricky questions for all cloud-based tools used by lawyers and clients daily. However, it effectively draws a line in the sand: attorneys must use enterprise-grade tools with strict "no training / zero retention" data policies if they want to maintain privilege.

**Precedent and Scope**
While some users worried this sets a dangerous precedent for AI use in the legal sphere, others correctly pointed out that this is a single criminal case ruled on by a district judge in the SDNY. It is highly fact-specific and does not serve as binding, nationwide precedent (like a SCOTUS ruling would), though it does serve as an early warning for modern legal tech protocols.

**The Takeaway:** Tech enthusiasts may view ChatGPT or Claude as an advanced notepad, but the law views consumer-grade AI platforms as third-party entities. If a client talks to AI without their lawyer's direction—especially on a platform that uses chat logs for training data—they are waiving their right to legally privileged confidentiality.

Cambridge builds ultra‑low‑power “brain‑like” memristor that could cut AI energy use

- What’s new: Researchers engineered a hafnium‑oxide thin film (doped with strontium and titanium) that acts as a highly stable, low‑energy memristor—reported in Science Advances.
- Why it matters: Neuromorphic chips that compute and store in the same place could reduce AI energy use by up to 70%. Today’s AI shuttles data between memory and compute, burning power.
- How it works: Instead of flaky conductive filaments, the device switches via tiny interface “p‑n junction” barriers inside the oxide, letting resistance change smoothly and predictably.
- Performance highlights:
  - Switching currents ~1,000,000× lower than some conventional oxide devices
  - Hundreds of distinct, stable conductance levels (good for analog in‑memory compute)
  - Endurance: tens of thousands of cycles; retention: ~1 day
  - Demonstrates spike‑timing dependent plasticity (a key biological learning rule)
- Caveats: Fabrication currently needs ~700°C—hotter than standard semiconductor processes—and retention is modest for some use cases.
- What’s next: Lower the processing temperature to make it fab‑friendly and integrate onto chip‑scale systems. If successful, this could be a major step toward practical, adaptive, low‑power neuromorphic hardware.
