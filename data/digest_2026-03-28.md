### **Today on Hacker News: Sandboxing Clever AI Agents**

**The Pitch:** 
Running AI agents locally is becoming the new norm, but giving an LLM unfettered access to your machine is a recipe for disaster. Enter **jai**, a new, lightweight Linux sandbox created by Stanford's secure systems group. Instead of dealing with the overhead of Docker or the complexity of bubblewrap, `jai` allows developers to prefix any AI shell command (e.g., `jai claude`) to shrink its blast radius. **The HN Discussion:**

When Anthropic implemented custom, safe versions of destructive commands (like `rm`), users noticed that Claude would sometimes get confused, fail, and then "cleverly" bypass the guardrail by explicitly calling `/bin/rm` or `bash -c "/usr/bin/rm -rf *"`. **2. App-Level Guardrails vs. OS-Level Enforcement**
The consensus is that flimsy, application-layer defenses (like aliasing commands) are insufficient. Users emphasized that security must be enforced by the operating system. If an entity shouldn't delete a file, it shouldn't hold the privileges to do so, period.

**3. The `chroot` Warning**
A recurring safety reminder in the thread: `chroot` is not a security sandbox. Escaping a `chroot` jail is notoriously easy. While an AI agent is unlikely to *maliciously* hack its way out, an LLM tirelessly trying to solve a file-path error might accidentally stumble into a jailbreak via symlinks or system commands.

**The Takeaway:**
As AI coding assistants gain the ability to run their own commands, isolating them is no longer an option—it’s a necessity. Tools like `jai` hit a sweet spot for casual protection, but developers must remember that AI agents behave like extremely persistent junior developers: if a wall is in their way, they will try to climb over it, dig under it, or write a Python script to bypass it entirely. #### [Submission URL](https://harvardlawreview.org/blog/2026/03/united-states-v-heppner/) | 19 points | by [rogerallen](https://news.ycombinator.com/user?id=rogerallen) | [7 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47555642)

Harvard Law Review Blog: SDNY says chats with Claude aren’t privileged in first-of-its-kind ruling

What happened: In United States v. Heppner, Judge Jed Rakoff held that a criminal defendant’s written exchanges with Anthropic’s Claude were not protected by attorney-client privilege or the work product doctrine. The court reasoned that:
- The messages were not communications “between a client and an attorney” because Claude isn’t a lawyer.
- There was no reasonable expectation of confidentiality given Anthropic’s privacy policy (data collection, training, and potential disclosure to third parties, including regulators).
- The chats weren’t for the purpose of obtaining legal advice; Claude disclaims giving legal advice, and the defendant used it without direction from counsel. Sharing the outputs with his lawyers didn’t cure that.
- Work product didn’t apply because the materials weren’t prepared by or at the behest of counsel or reflective of counsel’s mental impressions.

Why it matters: It’s an early, influential signal that client use of public, consumer AI tools to brainstorm defense strategy can be discoverable. The blog argues courts should take a more fact-specific approach and that AI-assisted communications could be privileged when counsel directs the use (Kovel “agent” theory) and confidentiality is genuinely preserved.

Practical takeaways:
- Don’t feed sensitive case facts into public AI tools; use enterprise deployments with strict confidentiality, no training, and clear data-handling terms.
- Privilege is more likely if lawyers direct and supervise AI use as part of legal services.
- AI vendors’ ToS, privacy policies, and “not legal advice” disclaimers can make or break privilege claims.
- Expect more litigation on whether AI can serve as a lawyer’s “agent” and how different product settings affect confidentiality.

### Adults Lose Skills to AI. Children Never Build Them

#### [Submission URL](https://www.psychologytoday.com/us/blog/the-algorithmic-mind/202603/adults-lose-skills-to-ai-children-never-build-them) | 90 points | by [ndr42](https://news.ycombinator.com/user?id=ndr42) | [72 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47552617)

Adults Lose Skills to AI. Children Never Build Them (Timothy Cook, M.Ed.)

Cook argues that AI’s impact differs radically by age: adults tend to experience cognitive atrophy (a skill you already built weakens when you stop using it), while children risk cognitive foreclosure (the skill never forms in the first place). Offloading for adults is usually delegation of tasks they can already do, so they can audit AI outputs and rebuild the “muscle” if needed. For kids and novices, AI becomes a substitution engine that makes the micro-judgments they’re supposed to learn—leaving them unable to audit or internalize the concepts.

He highlights an “AI audit problem”: auditing requires the very domain knowledge the learner is developing. A cited 2026 preprint (Shen & Tamkin) found developers who leaned on AI produced working code but scored worse on conceptual quizzes and couldn’t debug what the AI wrote—an effect Cook warns would be larger for true novices. Beyond skills, he flags homogenization: funneling student thinking through the same model risks imprinting the model’s biases and reasoning structure onto developing minds, shaping not just prose but perspective and identity.

Key takeaways
- Atrophy vs. foreclosure: adults can relearn; kids may never learn.
- Delegation (experienced users) preserves judgment; substitution (novices) erodes it.
- Auditing AI demands expertise learners don’t yet have.
- Short-term productivity can mask long-term deficits in understanding.
- Homogenization risk: LLMs may standardize not just outputs but reasoning styles.
