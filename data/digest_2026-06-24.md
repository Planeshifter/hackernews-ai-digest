### Puzzling Success of Overparameterization: Lottery Tickets or Escape Dimensions?

#### [Submission URL](https://infoscience.epfl.ch/entities/publication/9a49779b-f9f8-448d-b3d1-737c78455309) | 42 points | by [rbanffy](https://news.ycombinator.com/user?id=rbanffy) | [8 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48657481)

This preprint argues the popular “lottery ticket” story for why big neural nets work is misleading. Rather than wide models succeeding because they hide a lucky subnetwork that trains in isolation, the authors claim width helps by reshaping optimization.

Key points:
- Subnetworks aren’t independent: so-called “winning tickets” can be made to fail by perturbing the rest of the network, undermining the idea that gradient descent is just a parallel search over subnetworks.
- Reframe via loss-landscape geometry: increasing width adds optimization dimensions, making it easier for gradient descent to escape bad local minima.
- As width grows, bad minima become increasingly rare relative to good ones, improving trainability.
- Takeaway for practice: the apparent “redundancy” of large models is functional—extra parameters expand escape dimensions—suggesting we should refine pruning and lottery-ticket narratives in light of modern theory.

Authors: Flavio Martinelli, Johanni Brea, Wulfram Gerstner (preprint, CC BY).

Here is a daily digest summary of the Hacker News discussion regarding the preprint:

**The Death of the Lottery Ticket? Why Bigger Nets Actually Train Better**
A fascinating new preprint generated intense discussion today by taking aim at the popular "Lottery Ticket Hypothesis" in deep learning. The researchers argue that massive neural networks don't succeed simply because they hide a lucky, isolated sub-network. Instead, adding parameters fundamentally changes the geometry of the loss landscape, creating extra dimensions that allow gradient descent to escape bad local minima.

The Hacker News community dove deep into the math, history, and practical implications of this structural shift. Here are the top takeaways from the discussion:

*   **The Math Checks Out (and isn't entirely new):** Several readers pointed out that the topological argument aligns with older research (dating back to around 2014) regarding Hessian matrices in high-dimensional non-convex optimization. Statistically, treating the eigenvalues as a Bernoulli process shows that the probability of getting stuck entirely in a bad local minimum drops exponentially as the parameter count jumps into the billions.
*   **The True Mystery is "Double Descent":** While some users felt that "wider models equal fewer traps" is somewhat scientifically intuitive, they argued the conversation needs to focus on *double descent*. This is the heavily observed phenomenon where test error drops, then spikes badly right where model parameters perfectly interpolate the data, before sharply descending again as the model becomes massively overparameterized. 
*   **Regularization Beats Hand-Crafting:** An insightful sub-thread drew parallels to spline fitting and classic statistical modeling, noting that this trend dates back to classical ML texts like *The Elements of Statistical Learning* (2001). The modern consensus is clear: it is incredibly difficult to manually design a "just-right," limited-parameter model. It is far more robust and practical to massively overparameterize the model and use heavy regularization to let the computer find the optimization space. 
*   **Spatial Intuition over "Winning Tickets":** Users debated the original Lottery Ticket author's evolving stance on their own hypothesis. One commenter provided a helpful mental model for the geometry: a tiny model with an unlucky starting position has no meaningful gradient and must slowly slide a feature to the right place. A massively overparameterized model, however, basically "spawns" features everywhere at initialization, practically guaranteeing that some parameters start exactly where they need to be to lock onto a meaningful gradient immediately. 

**The Digest Verdict:** 
The community broadly agrees with the preprint's shift away from the "lottery ticket" narrative. The modern understanding of deep learning treats "redundancy" not as dead weight, but as functional scaffolding that fundamentally bends the optimization landscape in our favor.

### Qwen-AgentWorld: Language World Models for General Agents

#### [Submission URL](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.24597) | 198 points | by [ilreb](https://news.ycombinator.com/user?id=ilreb) | [55 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48654351)

Qwen-AgentWorld: language world models that simulate environments for general agents

- What’s new: Alibaba’s team introduces Qwen-AgentWorld, large “language world models” that predict next environment states from observations and actions. Two sizes are released: Qwen-AgentWorld-35B-A3B and Qwen-AgentWorld-397B-A17B. Trained on 10M+ interaction trajectories spanning 7 real-world domains, the models perform long chain-of-thought rollouts to simulate complex, agentic environments.

- How it works: A three-stage pipeline—CPT (injects state-transition dynamics from corpora and logs), SFT (teaches explicit next-state prediction), and RL (improves simulation fidelity using hybrid rubric-and-rule rewards). The team also unveils AgentWorldBench, built from real interaction traces of 5 frontier models across 9 established benchmarks, to evaluate language world models more realistically.

- Results: The paper reports that Qwen-AgentWorld significantly outperforms frontier baselines on AgentWorldBench. Beyond benchmarking, they show two uses:
  1) As a decoupled simulator to train agents with RL at scale across thousands of controllable environments, yielding gains that exceed training solely in real environments.
  2) As a unified agent foundation model, where world-model training serves as a warm start that boosts downstream performance across 7 agentic benchmarks.

- Why it matters: If robust, language-native world models could cut the cost and safety risk of real-environment interaction, accelerate agent training, and improve generalization across tasks—pushing toward more capable, sample-efficient general agents.

- Caveats to watch: Breadth beyond the 7 trained domains, sim-to-real transfer, compute/runtime for the 397B model, and how much of the reported gains depend on long chain-of-thought prompting. Code and benchmark links are provided in the paper.

In the thread, users explained that a regular LLM acts as a policy model (mapping a *current state* to an *action*). In contrast, Qwen-AgentWorld acts as an environment simulator (mapping a *current state* + *an action* mathematically to a *forecasted future state*, like generating the exact HTML of the next screen). 
*   **Enabling "Lookahead" Agents:** Commenters quickly identified the main benefit of this architecture: classical tree search. By allowing an agent to ask the world model, "What happens if I do this?", the agent can simulate the consequences of its actions, evaluate potential execution paths, and catch mistakes before deploying them in a real environment. 
*   **Simulation vs. Human "Dreaming":** A fascinating tangent emerged comparing these world models to human dreaming. Users discussed how humans use dreams to neurologically tidy up, surface subconscious plans, and pre-emptively simulate scenarios. Moving forward, having an AI "dream" (simulate future states based on generalized rules) could dramatically improve safety and logic execution without real-world risk.
*   **The Solution to the Data Wall:** An underlying theme in the discussion was the looming "data wall" (running out of high-quality human text on the internet). Users noted that robust world models are highly anticipated because they can generate infinite, high-fidelity synthetic data. By acting as a simulator, Qwen-AgentWorld allows agents to be trained via Reinforcement Learning at scale in a sandbox, rather than relying strictly on human-generated data. 
*   **Local Tinkering and Open Weights:** The release of open weights for the 35B model caused an immediate clamor for localized, compressed versions. Within hours, the community was sharing links to quantized GGUF formats (via Unsloth) and debugging load errors in tools like `llama.cpp`.
