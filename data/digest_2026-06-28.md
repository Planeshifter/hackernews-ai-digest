### Knowledge Distillation of Black-Box Large Language Models (2024)

#### [Submission URL](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.07013) | 117 points | by [babelfish](https://news.ycombinator.com/user?id=babelfish) | [23 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48712420)

Knowledge Distillation of Black-Box Large Language Models proposes Proxy-KD, a clever workaround for a core distillation roadblock: proprietary LLMs like GPT-4 are great teachers but don’t expose internals (logits, hidden states), so students can only learn from final text outputs. Proxy-KD first trains an intermediate “proxy” model on the black-box teacher’s outputs, then distills from that proxy to a smaller student using rich white-box signals the proxy can provide. In experiments, this two-step pipeline not only strengthens distillation from black-box teachers but also outperforms standard white-box KD baselines. The upshot: better small models without needing privileged access to commercial LLM internals—potentially a more scalable path for open models and on-device use. Paper: arXiv:2401.07013 (v2, Nov 2024), DOI: 10.48550/arXiv.2401.07013.

### Do LLMs pass the mirror test?

#### [Submission URL](https://blog.pascalschuster.de/article/do-llms-pass-the-mirror-test) | 73 points | by [thepasch](https://news.ycombinator.com/user?id=thepasch) | [64 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48710414)

Do LLMs pass the mirror test? A text-perturbation twist suggests… maybe

Summary:
- Pascal argues most “mirror tests” for LLMs are mis-specified, mirroring the classic visual test rather than targeting the model’s primary modality and context: text within a user-assistant chat.
- Inspired by Alexandra Horowitz’s olfactory mirror test for dogs, he edits the model’s own past messages—its “scent”—and watches for unprompted anomaly detection.
- Using Gemma 4 31B-IT in Google AI Studio (which lets you edit assistant turns), he subtly corrupts its earlier replies by replacing every “g” with “sg” (and stripping uppercase G). The model proceeds normally for two turns.
- Midway through drafting a third reply, the model’s visible thinking trace flags a recurring pattern of odd typos—essentially, “mine, but wrong.” Pascal likens this to the dog fixating on its altered scent.

Why it matters:
- This setup probes for spontaneous self-monitoring rather than explicit self-recognition: detecting discrepancies against an internal baseline while “busy” with another task.
- It doesn’t prove philosophical self-awareness, but it’s a compelling sign of on-the-fly anomaly detection—arguably what the mirror test really measures.

Caveats:
- Single model and perturbation; the detection surfaced in internal traces, not necessarily the final answer.
- Many closed models don’t expose thinking traces, and generality across tasks/edits is unproven.

Bottom line:
- If the mirror test is about noticing “me, but off,” then subtly corrupting a model’s own chat history is a clean, low-friction probe—and at least in this case, the model sniffed the canister.
