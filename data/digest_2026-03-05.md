How they measure it
- Map tasks to ~800 O*NET occupations.
- Start with task-level feasibility from Eloundou et al. (β=0, 0.5, 1 for speedups via LLMs).
- Overlay real-world usage from the Anthropic Economic Index, emphasizing automated/professional use.

Early read
- Adoption gap: Actual AI use is still a fraction of what’s theoretically feasible. Yet usage aligns with feasibility: 97% of observed tasks fall in β≥0.5, 68% in β=1, and just 3% in β=0.
- Growth headwinds: Occupations with higher observed exposure are projected by the BLS to grow less through 2034.
- Who’s most exposed: Older, female, more educated, and higher-paid workers tilt more exposed.

Past “susceptibility” scores (offshorability, robots) often overpredicted disruption. By tethering exposure to real usage, this framework may flag vulnerable occupations earlier and separate hype from labor-market signal. It’s early days, and many frictions (legal, workflow, verification) still damp diffusion, but the hiring slowdown among younger workers in exposed jobs is a canary worth watching.

### Can coding agents relicense open source through a "clean room" implementation?

#### [Submission URL](https://simonwillison.net/2026/Mar/5/chardet/) | 19 points | by [MindGods](https://news.ycombinator.com/user?id=MindGods) | [9 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47264043)

Headline: Can AI clean-room rewrites relicense open source? chardet becomes a test case

Simon Willison examines whether coding agents can produce legally defensible “clean-room” rewrites fast enough to justify a new license—and points to a live controversy around the long-standing Python library chardet.

What happened
- chardet, originally LGPL (2006, Mark Pilgrim; maintained by Dan Blanchard since 2012), shipped 7.0.0 as a “ground-up, MIT-licensed rewrite”—same name and API, promising big speed/accuracy gains.
- Pilgrim objected, arguing there’s “no right to relicense” under the LGPL because maintainers were extensively exposed to the original code; a “complete rewrite” claim is irrelevant without a proper clean-room separation.
- Blanchard replied that while traditional clean-room separation didn’t exist, independence can be shown by results: JPlag similarity between 7.0.0 and prior chardet releases was ~1.29% vs 5.x/6.x and 0.64% vs 1.1 (older inter-version similarities were 80–93%).
- He detailed the process: start from an empty repo, build tests first, use Claude Code with explicit instructions not to base work on copylefted code, and iterate via review. Repo artifacts include a step-by-step rewrite plan. One noted wrinkle: Claude referenced metadata/charsets.py from the old tree during planning.
- Additional complications: the maintainer’s decade of familiarity; the likelihood that the AI model was trained on chardet; and chardet itself was originally a manual Python port of Mozilla’s MPL-licensed detector. Reusing the same PyPI package name may also matter.

Why it matters
- This is a high-visibility microcosm of a bigger question: Can AI-assisted “clean-room” rewrites break copyleft inheritance and enable relicensing, especially when authors have prior exposure and models may have seen the source?
- The outcome could shape norms for AI-driven reimplementations, from process documentation and similarity testing to package naming and community trust.

Open questions
- Is demonstrated structural independence (via tools like JPlag) enough without strict personnel separation?
- Does model pretraining on the original code undermine a clean-room claim?
- Would releasing under a new package name change the legal or ethical calculus?
- How should projects handle legacy lineage (e.g., MPL-origin code) when attempting a fresh license?

Willison’s take: he’s tentatively leaning that the rewrite could be legitimate, but both sides have credible arguments—and this case may set important precedents for AI coding agents and open-source licensing.
