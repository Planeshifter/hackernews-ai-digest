TL;DR: A closer look at “open vs closed” LLM progress shows a mixed picture: on one headline index, open models look set to catch up by late 2026, but across 18 benchmarks the average lag has stayed roughly flat at just under five months, with most of the convergence driven by coding.

What’s new
- The author recreates the viral “open-source catch‑up” plot using Artificial Analysis’ Intelligence Index, then extends the method to 18 benchmarks (coding, math, agentic tasks, GPQA, MMLU‑Pro, LiveCodeBench, TerminalBench, etc.), building interactive frontier and box‑plot visualizations.

How the gap is measured
- For each benchmark and month: find the best open‑weights model’s score, then look back to when the closed‑source frontier first hit that level. The time difference is the “open frontier lag” in months. Plot over time and fit a trend line.

Key findings
- Intelligence Index alone: the lag shrinks notably from summer 2024; a linear extrapolation hits zero around Dec 3, 2026. Cue the tongue‑in‑cheek “open source apocalypse” joke.
- All 18 benchmarks together: the monthly box‑plots and mean trend are almost flat at just under five months—no clear collapse of the gap overall.
- Convergence is concentrated in coding: the coding index moved from ~15 months behind to ~1–2 months.
- Many other datasets show the gap holding steady or even widening modestly.

Takeaways
- Single benchmarks can be misleading; depending on which metric you pick, you can argue for near‑term parity or a persistent lag.
- Most of the visible catch‑up lately is in coding; outside of code, open models are not obviously closing the gap as fast.
- Measuring “overall LLM quality” remains tricky—different benchmarks tell different stories, and trend‑line extrapolations are fragile.

#### **1. The "200,000-Line Blob" vs. The "API"**
The core debate centered on how the math community should treat unreadable AI outputs. One user pointed to the difference between human-created formal math (which uses clean abstractions) and the possibility of an AI submitting a "200,000-line un-factored blob of code" to a formal proof library like Lean’s Mathlib.

In this view, if the machine verifies that the logic holds, the theorem is true, and we shouldn't have to re-prove it or understand its guts to use it—just as we don't re-prove Fermat's Little Theorem every time we apply it. 
*   **The Maintenance Argument:** Pragmatists pushed back, noting that merging a giant blob of unreadable code into a library like Mathlib creates massive maintenance burdens. Even if the machine says it’s true, if the code lacks structure, it is untrustworthy and unmaintainable as a living document.

#### **2. The Journey *Is* the Mathematics**
Echoing the original article, many commenters argued that the final theorem is rarely the point. Math is about the intermediate steps.
*   **Theory Building over Answers:** Users pointed out that reading the intermediate steps of unrelated papers often sparks new insights. One commenter brought up Évariste Galois: he developed techniques to solve degree-5 polynomial equations, but the *abstractions* he created gave rise to entirely new fields of mathematics. 
*   **The Problem is an Excuse:** Many mathematical theorems (like finding the minimum number of colors to draw a map) are essentially "pointless" in isolation. They serve as an excuse to invent new processes. If an AI gives us the answer via an unreadable black box, we get the destination but lose out on the journey, entirely stunting our ability to extrapolate and learn.

#### **3. Anthropocentrism vs. The "Machine God"**
The thread took an existential turn when debating whether the universe's mathematical truths are bound by human comprehension. 
*   **Beyond Human Limits:** What if the true boundaries of mathematics exist beyond the capabilities of the human mind? One user suggested it is inherently arrogant to assume all of mathematics must be reducible to something a human can understand. If AI can make intuitive leaps we can't follow—but which yield practical effects (like a breakthrough in quantum computing or code-breaking)—we may have to accept it. 
*   **Rejecting the Oracle:** Conversely, others vehemently rejected the idea of submitting to a "Machine God" that spits out gibberish answers. One user compared it to a medieval alchemist speaking to a magical oracle: if the oracle gives you gold but speaks in an incomprehensible language, you are richer, but you still haven't learned anything about the actual science of chemistry. 

#### **The Takeaway**
Is there a point to doing math if an AI can do it faster? One user perfectly encapsulated the consensus with a simple metaphor: **Robots can easily be built to cover 42 kilometers, but humans still run marathons.** 

Ultimately, the consensus suggests that while AI will provide incredible tools and verified "Lego blocks" for mathematicians to build upon, a proof that lacks human meaning is just a checkmark. Mathematics will remain an inherently human pursuit of understanding.

### Scaling Laws, Carefully

#### [Submission URL](https://lilianweng.github.io/posts/2026-06-24-scaling-laws/) | 14 points | by [tehnub](https://news.ycombinator.com/user?id=tehnub) | [8 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48689744)

This deep dive distills a decade of work on scaling laws—the surprisingly reliable rules-of-thumb that predict how model loss falls as you scale parameters (N), data (D), and compute (C ≈ 6ND). The punchline: on log-log plots, loss tends to trace straight lines, letting you forecast the payoffs of bigger models and larger datasets—and choose the best way to spend a fixed compute budget.

Key ideas
- Predictable power laws: Across tasks and architectures, test loss is well fit by terms like A/N^α + B/D^β + E. Architecture mostly shifts the curve up/down; the slope (exponent) looks like a property of the problem.
- Early theory and empirics: Classic results (Amari ’92) and broad studies (Hestness ’17) show power-law learning curves with three phases: data-starved, power-law, and irreducible-error. Small models plateau when data gets large.
- Joint modeling: Rosenfeld ’20 models loss jointly in N and D, enabling extrapolation from small sweeps to larger runs.
- Kaplan vs. Chinchilla, reconciled: Kaplan et al. (2020) suggested making models larger at fixed compute; DeepMind’s Chinchilla (2022) showed compute-optimal training uses much more data per parameter (rule-of-thumb: train on roughly tens of tokens per parameter), making smaller-but-well-trained models outperform under-trained giants. The apparent conflict comes from different data regimes (data-limited vs. data-rich).
- How to fit scaling laws in practice:
  1) Fix N, sweep D (or vice versa) to get exponents.
  2) IsoFLOP profiles: move along curves with constant ND to find the best N–D trade-off at fixed compute.
  3) Parametric fits: learn a small set of coefficients to predict loss across N and D.
- Why power laws? Intuitions point to spectral properties of data/gradients, bias–variance trade-offs, and aggregation over many weakly dependent factors.
- Caveats: Fits can be brittle—data dedup, tokenizer changes, optimizer tweaks, or shifting evaluation metrics move the curves. Regime changes (e.g., leaving the power-law region) can break extrapolations.

Why it matters
- Planning: A handful of small runs can forecast token budgets, model sizes, and expected loss for much larger training jobs.
- Efficiency: IsoFLOP analysis helps allocate compute between parameters and data to avoid training models that are too big for the available tokens.
- Reliability: Scaling laws remain one of the most actionable tools in modern DL—use them as guardrails, but watch for regime shifts and recipe drift.

**The Core Debate: Scaling Laws vs. The "Entropy Floor" of Language**
The conversation rapidly pivoted from the mathematical utility of scaling laws to their philosophical and practical limits—specifically, what happens when models hit the "irreducible error" phase mentioned in the article. 

Here are the central arguments from the thread:

*   **The Optimistic View (Scaling = Sustained Capability Gains):** 
    Users point out that scaling laws have been the most reliable predictor of AI capabilities over the past five years, suggesting skeptics should pay closer attention to them. While acknowledging that there is a theoretical limit to how low cross-entropy loss can go, this group argues that absolute loss numbers aren't the whole story. As models approach the "floor" of predictable language, the remaining irreducible errors are the highly complex, "difficult reasoning steps." Therefore, even minute fractional improvements in loss at this stage represent enormous leaps in actual model capability and reasoning. 
*   **The Skeptic View (Mathematical Caps on Intelligence):**
    Other commenters argue that the natural entropy of human language creates a hard, intractable limit on cross-entropy loss. Because language is inherently a game of "partial information" involving hidden human intents, a model can only ever approximate or guess asymptotically—it will always fail to predict certain tokens correctly. 
*   **Why Coding is Easier, and the Post-GPT-4 Plateau:**
    This "entropy floor" concept is used to explain current industry trends. One user notes that programming languages are highly tractable for current LLMs precisely because they have significantly less entropy than natural human language. Furthermore, some suggest this mathematical cap explains why we haven't seen a massive "step-function" leap in raw base-model capabilities since GPT-4. Instead of relying solely on brute-force scaling, the industry is increasingly leaning into specialized tooling, context-specific workarounds, and agentic workflows to get around these inherent mathematical limits. 

**Sub-theme: The Compute Cost of Context**
The discussion also touched on the friction between infinite scaling and hardware limits. Users noted that while gaining a linear increase in context length, the compute requirements (specifically for attention mechanisms) scale quadratically, resulting in massive bottlenecks for GPU resources.
