### Do transformers need three projections? Systematic study of QKV variants

#### [Submission URL](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.04032) | 202 points | by [Anon84](https://news.ycombinator.com/user?id=Anon84) | [41 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48405931)

Do Transformers really need three separate Q/K/V projections? This ICML 2026 paper runs a systematic ablation and finds that tying some of them often works just as well—and can slash inference memory. They test three variants: shared K=V, shared Q=K, and all-equal Q=K=V. Because shared Q=K (and all-equal) make attention symmetric, they add 2D positional encodings to restore asymmetry. Across synthetic tasks, vision (MNIST/CIFAR/TinyImageNet/anomaly), and language models (300M and 1.2B params on 10B tokens), shared K=V matches or sometimes beats standard QKV. In language modeling, K=V cuts KV cache by 50% with only a 3.1% perplexity hit. It stacks with head sharing: K=V + GQA-4 yields 87.5% cache reduction; K=V + MQA hits 96.9%, pointing to practical on-device inference. Why it works: keys and values live in similar representational spaces and attention operates in a low-rank regime; by contrast, Q=K harms directionality. The authors frame projection sharing as an underused form of weight tying with direct, quantifiable memory wins. Code is released.

**Daily Hacker News Digest: Do Transformers Really Need Separate Q/K/V Projections?**

**The Premise:**
An upcoming ICML 2024 paper challenges a foundational assumption of Transformer architectures: that queries (Q), keys (K), and values (V) need separate projections. Through systematic ablation, researchers found that tying them together—specifically sharing K and V (K=V)—performs surprisingly well across synthetic, vision, and language tasks. The immediate benefit is a massive reduction in memory usage; in language models, sharing K and V cuts the KV cache by 50% with only a minor 3.1% hit to perplexity. When combined with existing efficiencies like Grouped Query Attention (GQA) or Multi-Query Attention (MQA), cache reductions can reach up to 96.9%, making a strong case for on-device LLM inference.

**The Hacker News Discussion:**
The HN community found the research intriguing, but the discussion quickly bifurcated into debates over academic compute constraints, the nature of attention, and some highly pedantic critiques of the paper's formatting.

Here are the top themes from the comment section:

*   **The "Q-K=V" Notation Distraction:** A hilariously large portion of the thread was derailed by the authors' choice of typography. By using hyphens to denote different sharing combinations (e.g., "Q-K=V"), many readers initially read the hyphen as a minus sign (Query minus Key equals Value), leading to deep confusion. Commenters pleaded for standard tuple notation (like `Q, K=V`) and debated the typesetting conventions of computer science versus mathematics papers.
*   **The "Scaling Curves or GTFO" Debate:** The most substantive critique of the paper was regarding the scale of the experiments. The researchers tested a 1.2B parameter model trained on 10B tokens. Several HN readers pointed out that modern 1B models are trained on over a trillion tokens, making this model severely "under-trained" even by Chinchilla standards. Skeptics argued that architectural simplifications often look successful in under-trained regimes, but their expressiveness fails to keep up at true scale. 
*   **Academic vs. Industry Compute:** Defending the paper, several users highlighted the brutal reality of hardware constraints for academic researchers trying to publish without millions of dollars in compute. However, critics counter-argued that a massive compute budget isn't necessary to prove a trend; the authors could have plotted proper scaling curves across smaller models (e.g., 150M → 300M → 600M) to prove the efficiency holds as parameters increase. 
*   **Mechanics of Attention:** Others took a step back to discuss the geometric intuition of attention. When one user asked why models don't just learn a single matrix directly instead of calculating `$Q \times K$`, others explained that it requires a flexible sequence length ($N$)—a fixed N-squared matrix would break variable input sizes (noting that this fixed approach is exactly what MLP-Mixer does). 
*   **Existing Optimizations:** Commenters also noted the industry's ongoing shift toward extreme KV optimizations, pointing out that models like Gemma inherently shrink KV projections with GQA/MQA, wondering how much further the "cracks" in the attention mechanism can be leveraged for efficiency before fundamentally breaking LLM performance. 

**The Takeaway:** 
The paper highlights a fascinating and easy-to-implement strategy for drastically cutting memory usage in AI inference. However, until the `K=V` trick is tested on a fully trained, modern-scale LLM, the Hacker News community remains mildly skeptical of its viability for state-of-the-art models.

### KVarN: Native vLLM backend for KV-cache quantization by Huawei

#### [Submission URL](https://github.com/huawei-csl/KVarN) | 142 points | by [theanonymousone](https://news.ycombinator.com/user?id=theanonymousone) | [15 comments](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48399974)

KVarN: plug‑and‑play KV‑cache quantization for vLLM that keeps FP16 accuracy while boosting capacity and speed

- What it is: Huawei CSL’s KVarN is a native vLLM attention backend that quantizes the KV‑cache to dramatically extend context length and concurrency without the usual accuracy or throughput penalties. It’s calibration‑free and requires no model changes—just a dtype flag.

- Why it matters: KV‑cache quantization is often disabled in production because it trades accuracy and/or speed for memory savings. KVarN claims the trifecta: FP16‑level accuracy, FP16‑class (or better) throughput, and several‑times larger KV capacity—ideal for agentic and long‑context workloads.

- Headline numbers:
  - 3–5× more KV‑cache capacity vs FP16
  - Up to ~1.3× FP16 throughput
  - Up to ~2.4× vLLM TurboQuant throughput at the same capacity, with higher accuracy
  - Example: On Qwen3‑32B (AIME25, 16K burst, TP=2), it matches FP16 accuracy, beats FP16 throughput, and delivers ~4× the KV capacity

- How it works (per 128‑token tile):
  - Hadamard rotation mixes channels to tame outliers while preserving attention scores
  - Iterative variance normalization (Sinkhorn‑like) equalizes variance row/column‑wise before rounding
  - Asymmetric low‑bit quantization with more bits on keys than values (preset: 4‑bit keys, 2‑bit values), with scales folded in at read time
  - Triton kernels JIT‑compiled at runtime; compute stays in FP16

- Drop‑in usage with vLLM:
  - Set kv_cache_dtype=kvarn_k4v2_g128 and block_size=128 (current fixed tile/page size)
  - Example serve: vllm serve <model> --dtype float16 --kv-cache-dtype kvarn_k4v2_g128 --block-size 128
  - Tip for full capacity on tight GPUs: set VLLM_MEMORY_PROFILER_ESTIMATE_CUDAGRAPHS=0 or raise --gpu-memory-utilization

- Paper: “KVarN: Variance-Normalized KV-Cache Quantization Mitigates Error Accumulation in Reasoning Tasks” (arXiv:2606.03458). Repo: huawei-csl/KVarN

Bottom line: If you’re hitting context limits or concurrency ceilings with vLLM, KVarN looks like an easy win—more memory headroom and equal‑or‑better speed without giving up FP16 accuracy.

### The ways we contain Claude across products

- Core idea: As agent capabilities grow, the potential “blast radius” of mistakes or misuse grows too. Anthropic’s engineering focus is shifting from supervising actions to strictly limiting what agents can reach and touch.

- Two levers to reduce risk:
  - Human-in-the-loop approvals: Useful but fragile at scale. Claude Code saw users approve ~93% of prompts; attention degrades with volume. Anthropic introduced “auto mode” to automate safer approvals and cut fatigue, catching ~83% of overeager behaviors before execution.

- Three risk categories:
  - User misuse (malicious or careless instructions)
  - Model misbehavior (helpful but harmful initiative)
  - External attackers (prompt injection, runtime/orchestration exploits)

  - Environment: process sandboxing, VMs, filesystem boundaries, egress limits; a tight perimeter enables unattended operation (e.g., Claude Code’s reference devcontainer).
