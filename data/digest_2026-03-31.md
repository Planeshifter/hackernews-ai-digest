Here is what the HN community is saying about Cohere Transcribe:

**1. The "Plausible Hallucination" Problem & The Flaw of WER**
A major part of the discussion centered around how modern AI models—both in ASR and OCR (Optical Character Recognition)—handle ambiguous data. 
*   **The Xerox Effect:** Users drew parallels to a famous Xerox scanner bug where the OCR software confidently substituted incorrect but mathematically plausible numbers on scanned documents. The fear is that ASR does the exact same thing with garbled audio.
*   **Optimizing for the Wrong Metric:** Commenters criticized the industry-standard Word Error Rate (WER) metric. To achieve a low WER, models are incentivized to "make things up" when audio is unclear rather than simply outputting `[unintelligible]` or `[pause]`. The community consensus is that a "safe fail" (admitting it can't understand a word) is vastly preferable to confidently generating a hallucinated, incorrect transcription that requires human double-checking.

**2. Missing Crucial Production Features**
While Cohere’s raw transcription accuracy was praised, developers pointed out that raw text isn't enough for production environments.
*   **Timestamps and Diarization:** The model currently lacks word-level timestamps and speaker diarization (identifying *who* said *what*). Users noted that this is a widespread issue in the commercial space, with even giants like AWS, Google (Chirp), and OpenAI (Whisper) struggling to keep timestamps synced or avoiding hallucinations during long silences.
*   **The Workarounds:** Some users suggested alternative models for these specific needs, such as ElevenLabs for diarization or Mistral/Voxtral for timestamps. Others speculated whether community-built tools like WhisperX could eventually be adapted to wrap Cohere's model to add these features.
*   **No Custom Prompting:** Disappointment was expressed that the model doesn't currently support custom vocabularies or prompting—a feature highly valued in multi-modal LLMs (like GPT-4o) to ensure correct spelling of specific names, acronyms, or company terminology.

**3. Real-World Testing: Accents and Edge Cases**
Several users shared their own independent tests, proving that benchmark leadership doesn't always translate to direct real-world superiority.
*   **The British Postcode Test:** One developer ran an internal test using 250 recordings of people speaking British postcodes in various regional accents. In this specific test, Soniox and ElevenLabs took the top spots, while Cohere tied for the middle of the pack alongside AssemblyAI and Deepgram. 
*   **Foreign Accents:** Another recurrent issue raised was that models trained perfectly on native speech often completely break down on foreign accents (e.g., a French person speaking English), frequently hallucinating random phrases entirely unrelated to the topic. 

**4. Open Weights vs. Open Source**
There was a brief semantic debate regarding Cohere's use of the term "open." Users clarified that this is an "open weights" release, not fully open source. However, developers are already making use of the weights, noting that the community has already converted the model to ONNX format to allow for efficient CPU inference, bypassing the need for expensive GPUs.

**The Bottom Line:**
The Hacker News community views Cohere's Transcribe as a highly competent, welcome addition to the local-inference ecosystem, bolstered by Cohere's growing reputation for solid, reliable engineering. However, developers remain wary of benchmark-chasing. For Transcribe to truly unseat Whisper in the trenches, it will need to tackle complex real-world variables—foreign accents, speaker diarization, accurate timestamping, and the ability to safely admit when audio is simply unintelligible.

**The Core Debate: Is a "General" Time-Series Model Even Possible?**
The discussion kicked off with skepticism about the underlying premise of a "foundation model" for time-series data. A major theme in the comments was comparing traditional time-series methods (ARIMA, GARCH, ARMA) to neural networks:

In contrast, mixing chaotic financial data with predictable sensor data might not yield the same cross-domain benefits, raising concerns about overfitting. 
*   **Shared Underlying Causes:** Defenders of the foundation model approach suggested that disparate time series *do* share underlying causal structures—such as human behavior, holidays, and weather patterns (e.g., the stock market "taking the stairs up and the elevator down")—which a foundation model can learn to recognize.

*   **Predictable vs. Chaotic Output:** Commenters highlighted the limits of such models. While they are expected to excel at predictable, trend-heavy data (like actuarial tables, mortality rates, or structural engineering sensors), users warned against trying to use them for intraday crypto trading or 0-DTE option gambling, where the data is fundamentally chaotic and event-driven. 
*   **Multi-Variate Complexity:** Finally, users pointed out that while TimesFM may be great at predicting a single time series, predicting the complex correlation matrix *between* multiple time series remains incredibly difficult.
